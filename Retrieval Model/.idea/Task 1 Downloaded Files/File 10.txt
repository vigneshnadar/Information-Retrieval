     <p>
      In
      <b>
       passive solar building design
      </b>
      , windows, walls, and floors are made to collect, store, and distribute
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">
       solar energy
      </a>
      in the form of heat in the winter and reject solar heat in the summer. This is called passive solar design because, unlike active
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_heating" title="Solar heating">
       solar heating
      </a>
      systems, it does not involve the use of mechanical and electrical devices.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The key to design a passive solar building is to best take advantage of the local
      <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">
       climate
      </a>
      performing an accurate
      <a href="/wiki/Site_analysis" title="Site analysis">
       site analysis
      </a>
      . Elements to be considered include window placement and size, and
      <a href="/wiki/Glazing_(window)" title="Glazing (window)">
       glazing
      </a>
      type,
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_insulation" title="Thermal insulation">
       thermal insulation
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_mass" title="Thermal mass">
       thermal mass
      </a>
      , and shading.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Passive solar design techniques can be applied most easily to new buildings, but existing buildings can be adapted or "retrofitted".
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Passive_energy_gain">
       Passive energy gain
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_solar_building_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Passive energy gain">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:321px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Illust_passive_solar_d1.gif">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="216" data-file-width="319" height="216" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/49/Illust_passive_solar_d1.gif" width="319"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Illust_passive_solar_d1.gif" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Elements of passive solar design, shown in a direct gain application
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      <i>
       Passive solar
      </i>
      technologies use
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">
       sunlight
      </a>
      without active mechanical systems (as contrasted to
      <a href="/wiki/Active_solar" title="Active solar">
       active solar
      </a>
      ). Such technologies convert sunlight into usable heat (in water, air, and thermal mass), cause air-movement for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ventilating" title="Ventilating">
       ventilating
      </a>
      , or future use, with little use of other energy sources. A common example is a
      <a href="/wiki/Sunroom#Solarium" title="Sunroom">
       solarium
      </a>
      on the
      <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
       equator
      </a>
      -side of a building.
      <a href="/wiki/Passive_cooling" title="Passive cooling">
       Passive cooling
      </a>
      is the use of the same design principles to reduce summer cooling requirements.
     </p>
     <p>
      Some passive systems use a small amount of conventional energy to control dampers, shutters, night insulation, and other devices that enhance solar energy collection, storage, and use, and reduce undesirable
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_transfer" title="Heat transfer">
       heat transfer
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Passive solar technologies include direct and indirect
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_gain" title="Solar gain">
       solar gain
      </a>
      for space heating,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_hot_water" title="Solar hot water">
       solar water heating
      </a>
      systems based on the
      <a href="/wiki/Thermosiphon" title="Thermosiphon">
       thermosiphon
      </a>
      , use of
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_mass" title="Thermal mass">
       thermal mass
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phase_Change_Material" title="Phase Change Material">
       phase-change materials
      </a>
      for slowing indoor air temperature swings,
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_cooker" title="Solar cooker">
       solar cookers
      </a>
      , the
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_chimney" title="Solar chimney">
       solar chimney
      </a>
      for enhancing natural ventilation, and
      <a href="/wiki/Earth_sheltering" title="Earth sheltering">
       earth sheltering
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      More widely, passive solar technologies include the
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_furnace" title="Solar furnace">
       solar furnace
      </a>
      and
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_forge&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Solar forge (page does not exist)">
       solar forge
      </a>
      , but these typically require some external energy for aligning their concentrating mirrors or receivers, and historically have not proven to be practical or cost effective for widespread use. 'Low-grade' energy needs, such as space and water heating, have proven, over time, to be better applications for passive use of solar energy.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="As_a_science">
       As a science
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_solar_building_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: As a science">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific" title="Scientific">
       scientific
      </a>
      basis for passive solar building design has been developed from a combination of
      <a href="/wiki/Climatology" title="Climatology">
       climatology
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">
       thermodynamics
      </a>
      (particularly
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_transfer" title="Heat transfer">
       heat transfer
      </a>
      :
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conduction_(heat)" title="Conduction (heat)">
       conduction (heat)
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">
       convection
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">
       electromagnetic radiation
      </a>
      ),
      <a href="/wiki/Fluid_mechanics" title="Fluid mechanics">
       fluid mechanics
      </a>
      /
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_convection" title="Natural convection">
       natural convection
      </a>
      (passive movement of air and water without the use of electricity, fans or pumps), and human
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_comfort" title="Thermal comfort">
       thermal comfort
      </a>
      based on
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_index" title="Heat index">
       heat index
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Psychrometrics" title="Psychrometrics">
       psychrometrics
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy" title="Enthalpy">
       enthalpy
      </a>
      control for buildings to be inhabited by humans or animals,
      <a href="/wiki/Sunroom" title="Sunroom">
       sunrooms
      </a>
      , solariums, and
      <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">
       greenhouses
      </a>
      for raising plants.
     </p>
     <p>
      Specific attention is divided into: the site, location and solar orientation of the building, local
      <a href="/wiki/Sun_path" title="Sun path">
       sun path
      </a>
      , the prevailing level of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insolation" title="Insolation">
       insolation
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
       latitude
      </a>
      /sunshine/clouds/
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">
       precipitation
      </a>
      ), design and construction quality/materials, placement/size/type of windows and walls, and incorporation of solar-energy-storing
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_mass" title="Thermal mass">
       thermal mass
      </a>
      with
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_capacity" title="Heat capacity">
       heat capacity
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      While these considerations may be directed toward any building, achieving an ideal optimized cost/performance solution requires careful,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holistic" title="Holistic">
       holistic
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/System_integration" title="System integration">
       system integration
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">
       engineering
      </a>
      of these scientific principles.
      <a href="/wiki/History_of_passive_solar_building_design" title="History of passive solar building design">
       Modern refinements
      </a>
      through computer modeling (such as the comprehensive U.S. Department of Energy "Energy Plus"
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a href="/wiki/Building_energy_simulation" title="Building energy simulation">
       building energy simulation
      </a>
      software), and application of decades of lessons learned (since the 1970s energy crisis) can achieve significant energy savings and reduction of environmental damage, without sacrificing functionality or aesthetics.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fs110_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-fs110-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In fact, passive-solar design features such as a greenhouse/sunroom/solarium can greatly enhance the livability, daylight, views, and value of a home, at a low cost per unit of space.
     </p>
     <p>
      Much has been learned about passive solar building design since the 1970s energy crisis. Many unscientific, intuition-based expensive construction experiments have attempted and failed to achieve
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zero_energy_building" title="Zero energy building">
       zero energy
      </a>
      - the total elimination of heating-and-cooling energy bills.
     </p>
     <p>
      Passive solar building construction may not be difficult or expensive (using off-the-shelf existing materials and technology), but the scientific passive solar building design is a non-trivial engineering effort that requires significant study of previous counter-intuitive lessons learned, and time to enter, evaluate, and iteratively refine the
      <a href="/wiki/Building_energy_simulation" title="Building energy simulation">
       simulation
      </a>
      input and output.
     </p>
     <p>
      One of the most useful post-construction evaluation tools has been the use of
      <a href="/wiki/Thermography" title="Thermography">
       thermography
      </a>
      using digital
      <a href="/wiki/Thermographic_camera" title="Thermographic camera">
       thermal imaging cameras
      </a>
      for a formal quantitative scientific
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_audit" title="Energy audit">
       energy audit
      </a>
      . Thermal imaging can be used to document areas of poor thermal performance such as the negative thermal impact of roof-angled glass or a skylight on a cold winter night or hot summer day.
     </p>
     <p>
      The scientific lessons learned over the last three decades have been captured in sophisticated comprehensive
      <a href="/wiki/Building_energy_simulation" title="Building energy simulation">
       building energy simulation
      </a>
      computer software systems (like U.S. DOE Energy Plus).
     </p>
     <p>
      Scientific passive solar building design with quantitative
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cost_benefit" title="Cost benefit">
       cost benefit
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Product_optimization" title="Product optimization">
       product optimization
      </a>
      is not easy for a novice. The level of complexity has resulted in ongoing bad-architecture, and many intuition-based, unscientific construction experiments that disappoint their designers and waste a significant portion of their construction budget on inappropriate ideas.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The economic motivation for scientific design and engineering is significant. If it had been applied comprehensively to new building construction beginning in 1980 (based on 1970s lessons learned), America could be saving over $250,000,000 per year on expensive energy and related pollution today.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Since 1979, Passive Solar Building Design has been a critical element of achieving
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zero_energy_building" title="Zero energy building">
       zero energy
      </a>
      by educational institution experiments, and governments around the world, including the U.S. Department of Energy, and the energy research scientists that they have supported for decades. The
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cost_effective" title="Cost effective">
       cost effective
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Proof_of_concept" title="Proof of concept">
       proof of concept
      </a>
      was established decades ago, but
      <a href="/wiki/Cultural_assimilation" title="Cultural assimilation">
       cultural assimilation
      </a>
      into architecture, construction trades, and building-owner
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decision_making" title="Decision making">
       decision making
      </a>
      has been very slow and difficult to change.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The new terms "Architectural Science" and "Architectural Technology" are being added to some schools of Architecture, with a future goal of teaching the above scientific and energy-engineering principles.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="The_solar_path_in_passive_design">
       The solar path in passive design
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_solar_building_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The solar path in passive design">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Solar_altitude.svg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="497" data-file-width="713" height="209" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Solar_altitude.svg/300px-Solar_altitude.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Solar_altitude.svg/450px-Solar_altitude.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Solar_altitude.svg/600px-Solar_altitude.svg.png 2x" width="300"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Solar_altitude.svg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Solar altitude over a year; latitude based on
        <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">
         New York
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">
         New York
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main articles:
      <a href="/wiki/Sun_path" title="Sun path">
       Sun path
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Position_of_the_Sun" title="Position of the Sun">
       Position of the Sun
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      The ability to achieve these goals simultaneously is fundamentally dependent on the seasonal variations in the sun's path throughout the day.
     </p>
     <p>
      This occurs as a result of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclination" title="Inclination">
       inclination
      </a>
      of the Earth's axis of rotation in relation to its
      <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">
       orbit
      </a>
      . The
      <a href="/wiki/Sun_path" title="Sun path">
       sun path
      </a>
      is unique for any given latitude.
     </p>
     <p>
      In Northern Hemisphere non-tropical latitudes farther than 23.5 degrees from the equator:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       The sun will reach its
       <a href="/wiki/Noon" title="Noon">
        highest point
       </a>
       toward the south (in the direction of the equator)
      </li>
      <li>
       As winter
       <a href="/wiki/Solstice" title="Solstice">
        solstice
       </a>
       approaches, the
       <a href="/wiki/Azimuth" title="Azimuth">
        angle
       </a>
       at which the sun
       <a href="/wiki/Sunrise" title="Sunrise">
        rises
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Sunset" title="Sunset">
        sets
       </a>
       progressively moves further toward the South and the daylight hours will become shorter
      </li>
      <li>
       The opposite is noted in summer where the sun will rise and set further toward the North and the daylight hours will lengthen
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      The converse is observed in the Southern Hemisphere, but the sun rises to the east and sets toward the west regardless of which hemisphere you are in.
     </p>
     <p>
      In equatorial regions at less than 23.5 degrees, the position of the sun at
      <a href="/wiki/Noon" title="Noon">
       solar noon
      </a>
      will oscillate from north to south and back again during the year.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In regions closer than 23.5 degrees from either north-or-south pole, during summer the sun will trace a complete circle in the sky without setting whilst it will never appear above the horizon six months later, during the height of winter.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The 47-degree difference in the altitude of the sun at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_noon" title="Solar noon">
       solar noon
      </a>
      between winter and summer forms the basis of passive solar design. This information is combined with local climatic data (
      <a href="/wiki/Degree_day" title="Degree day">
       degree day
      </a>
      ) heating and cooling requirements to determine at what time of the year solar gain will be beneficial for
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_comfort" title="Thermal comfort">
       thermal comfort
      </a>
      , and when it should be blocked with shading. By strategic placement of items such as glazing and shading devices, the percent of solar gain entering a building can be controlled throughout the year.
     </p>
     <p>
      One
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passive_solar" title="Passive solar">
       passive solar
      </a>
      sun path design problem is that although the sun is in the same relative position six weeks before, and six weeks after, the solstice, due to "thermal lag" from the
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_mass" title="Thermal mass">
       thermal mass
      </a>
      of the Earth, the temperature and solar gain requirements are quite different before and after the summer or winter solstice. Movable shutters, shades, shade screens, or window quilts can accommodate day-to-day and hour-to-hour solar gain and insulation requirements.
     </p>
     <p>
      Careful arrangement of rooms completes the passive solar design. A common recommendation for residential dwellings is to place living areas facing solar noon and sleeping quarters on the opposite side.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_8-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      A
      <a href="/wiki/Heliodon" title="Heliodon">
       heliodon
      </a>
      is a traditional movable light device used by architects and designers to help model sun path effects. In modern times, 3D computer graphics can visually simulate this data, and calculate performance predictions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fs110_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-fs110-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Passive_solar_heat_transfer_principles">
       Passive solar heat transfer principles
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_solar_building_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Passive solar heat transfer principles">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Personal
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_comfort" title="Thermal comfort">
       thermal comfort
      </a>
      is a function of personal health factors (medical, psychological, sociological and situational), ambient air temperature,
      <a href="/wiki/Mean_radiant_temperature" title="Mean radiant temperature">
       mean radiant temperature
      </a>
      , air movement (
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_chill" title="Wind chill">
       wind chill
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">
       turbulence
      </a>
      ) and
      <a href="/wiki/Relative_humidity" title="Relative humidity">
       relative humidity
      </a>
      (affecting human
      <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">
       evaporative
      </a>
      cooling).
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_transfer" title="Heat transfer">
       Heat transfer
      </a>
      in buildings occurs through
      <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">
       convection
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_conduction" title="Heat conduction">
       conduction
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_radiation" title="Thermal radiation">
       thermal radiation
      </a>
      through roof, walls, floor and windows.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Convective_heat_transfer">
       Convective heat transfer
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_solar_building_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Convective heat transfer">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Convective_heat_transfer" title="Convective heat transfer">
       Convective heat transfer
      </a>
      can be beneficial or detrimental. Uncontrolled air infiltration from poor
      <a href="/wiki/Weatherization" title="Weatherization">
       weatherization
      </a>
      / weatherstripping / draft-proofing can contribute up to 40% of heat loss during winter;
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      however, strategic placement of operable windows or vents can enhance convection, cross-ventilation, and summer cooling when the outside air is of a comfortable temperature and
      <a href="/wiki/Relative_humidity" title="Relative humidity">
       relative humidity
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Filtered
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_recovery_ventilation" title="Energy recovery ventilation">
       energy recovery ventilation
      </a>
      systems may be useful to eliminate undesirable humidity, dust, pollen, and microorganisms in unfiltered ventilation air.
     </p>
     <p>
      Natural convection causing
      <a href="/wiki/Stack_effect" title="Stack effect">
       rising
      </a>
      warm air and falling cooler air can result in an uneven stratification of heat. This may cause uncomfortable variations in temperature in the upper and lower conditioned space, serve as a method of venting hot air, or be designed in as a natural-convection air-flow loop for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passive_solar" title="Passive solar">
       passive solar
      </a>
      heat distribution and temperature equalization. Natural human cooling by
      <a href="/wiki/Perspiration" title="Perspiration">
       perspiration
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">
       evaporation
      </a>
      may be facilitated through natural or forced convective air movement by fans, but ceiling fans can disturb the stratified insulating air layers at the top of a room, and accelerate heat transfer from a hot attic, or through nearby windows. In addition, high
      <a href="/wiki/Relative_humidity" title="Relative humidity">
       relative humidity
      </a>
      inhibits evaporative cooling by humans.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Radiative_heat_transfer">
       Radiative heat transfer
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_solar_building_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Radiative heat transfer">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The main source of
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_transfer" title="Heat transfer">
       heat transfer
      </a>
      is
      <a href="/wiki/Radiant_energy" title="Radiant energy">
       radiant energy
      </a>
      , and the primary source is the sun. Solar radiation occurs predominantly through the roof and windows (but also through walls).
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_radiation" title="Thermal radiation">
       Thermal radiation
      </a>
      moves from a warmer surface to a cooler one. Roofs receive the majority of the solar radiation delivered to a house. A
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cool_roof" title="Cool roof">
       cool roof
      </a>
      , or
      <a href="/wiki/Green_roof" title="Green roof">
       green roof
      </a>
      in addition to a
      <a href="/wiki/Radiant_barrier" title="Radiant barrier">
       radiant barrier
      </a>
      can help prevent your attic from becoming hotter than the peak summer outdoor air temperature
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      (see
      <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">
       albedo
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Absorbance" title="Absorbance">
       absorptivity
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Emissivity" title="Emissivity">
       emissivity
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reflectivity" title="Reflectivity">
       reflectivity
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      Windows are a ready and predictable site for
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_radiation" title="Thermal radiation">
       thermal radiation
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fs18a_13-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-fs18a-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Energy from radiation can move into a window in the day time, and out of the same window at night. Radiation uses
      <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">
       photons
      </a>
      to transmit
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_waves" title="Electromagnetic waves">
       electromagnetic waves
      </a>
      through a vacuum, or translucent medium. Solar heat gain can be significant even on cold clear days. Solar heat gain through windows can be reduced by
      <a href="/wiki/Insulated_glazing" title="Insulated glazing">
       insulated glazing
      </a>
      , shading, and orientation. Windows are particularly difficult to insulate compared to roof and walls.
      <a href="/wiki/Convective_heat_transfer" title="Convective heat transfer">
       Convective heat transfer
      </a>
      through and around
      <a href="/wiki/Window_covering" title="Window covering">
       window coverings
      </a>
      also degrade its insulation properties.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fs18a_13-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-fs18a-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      When shading windows, external shading is more effective at reducing heat gain than internal
      <a href="/wiki/Window_covering" title="Window covering">
       window coverings
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fs18a_13-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-fs18a-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Western and eastern sun can provide warmth and lighting, but are vulnerable to overheating in summer if not shaded. In contrast, the low midday sun readily admits light and warmth during the winter, but can be easily shaded with appropriate length overhangs or angled louvres during summer and leaf bearing summer shade trees which shed their leaves in the fall. The amount of radiant heat received is related to the location
      <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
       latitude
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">
       altitude
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Cloud_cover" title="Cloud cover">
       cloud cover
      </a>
      , and seasonal / hourly
      <a href="/wiki/Angle_of_incidence_(optics)" title="Angle of incidence (optics)">
       angle of incidence
      </a>
      (see
      <a href="/wiki/Sun_path" title="Sun path">
       Sun path
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Lambert%27s_cosine_law" title="Lambert's cosine law">
       Lambert's cosine law
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      Another passive solar design principle is that thermal energy can be
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_mass" title="Thermal mass">
       stored
      </a>
      in certain building materials and released again when heat gain eases to stabilize
      <a href="/wiki/Diurnal_temperature_variation" title="Diurnal temperature variation">
       diurnal
      </a>
      (day/night) temperature variations. The complex interaction of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermodynamic" title="Thermodynamic">
       thermodynamic
      </a>
      principles can be
      <a href="/wiki/Counterintuitive" title="Counterintuitive">
       counterintuitive
      </a>
      for first-time designers. Precise
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Building_Information_Modeling" title="Building Information Modeling">
       computer modeling
      </a>
      can help avoid costly construction experiments.
     </p>

      </li>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Design_elements_for_residential_buildings_in_temperate_climates">
       Design elements for residential buildings in temperate climates
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_solar_building_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Design elements for residential buildings in temperate climates">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Placement of room-types, internal doors and walls, and equipment in the house.
      </li>
      <li>
       Orienting the building to face the equator (or a few degrees to the East to capture the morning sun)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       Extending the building dimension along the east/west axis
      </li>
      <li>
       Adequately sizing windows to face the midday sun in the winter, and be shaded in the summer.
      </li>
      <li>
       Minimising windows on other sides, especially western windows
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fs18a_13-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-fs18a-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       Erecting correctly sized, latitude-specific roof overhangs,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or shading elements (shrubbery, trees, trellises, fences, shutters, etc.)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       Using the appropriate amount and type of
       <a href="/wiki/Building_insulation" title="Building insulation">
        insulation
       </a>
       including radiant barriers and bulk insulation to minimise seasonal excessive heat gain or loss
      </li>
      <li>
       Using
       <a href="/wiki/Thermal_mass" title="Thermal mass">
        thermal mass
       </a>
       to store excess solar energy during the winter day (which is then re-radiated during the night)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      The precise amount of equator-facing glass and thermal mass should be based on careful consideration of latitude, altitude, climatic conditions, and heating/cooling
      <a href="/wiki/Degree_day" title="Degree day">
       degree day
      </a>
      requirements.
     </p>
     <p>
      Factors that can degrade thermal performance:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Deviation from ideal orientation and north/south/east/west aspect ratio
      </li>
      <li>
       Excessive glass area ("over-glazing") resulting in overheating (also resulting in glare and fading of soft furnishings) and heat loss when ambient air temperatures fall
      </li>
      <li>
       Installing glazing where solar gain during the day and thermal losses during the night cannot be controlled easily e.g. West-facing, angled glazing, skylights
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       Thermal losses through non-insulated or unprotected glazing
      </li>
      <li>
       Lack of adequate shading during seasonal periods of high solar gain (especially on the West wall)
      </li>
      <li>
       Incorrect application of
       <a href="/wiki/Thermal_mass" title="Thermal mass">
        thermal mass
       </a>
       to modulate daily temperature variations
      </li>
      <li>
       Open staircases leading to unequal distribution of warm air between upper and lower floors as warm air rises
      </li>
      <li>
       High building surface area to volume - Too many corners
      </li>
      <li>
       Inadequate
       <a href="/wiki/Weatherization" title="Weatherization">
        weatherization
       </a>
       leading to high air infiltration
      </li>
      <li>
       Lack of, or incorrectly installed,
       <a href="/wiki/Radiant_barrier" title="Radiant barrier">
        radiant barriers
       </a>
       during the hot season. (See also
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cool_roof" title="Cool roof">
        cool roof
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Green_roof" title="Green roof">
        green roof
       </a>
       )
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Building_insulation" title="Building insulation">
        Insulation materials
       </a>
       that are not matched to the main mode of heat transfer (e.g. undesirable convective/conductive/radiant
       <a href="/wiki/Heat_transfer" title="Heat transfer">
        heat transfer
       </a>
       )
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Efficiency_and_economics_of_passive_solar_heating">
       Efficiency and economics of passive solar heating
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_solar_building_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Efficiency and economics of passive solar heating">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Technische_Universit%C3%A4t_Darmstadt_-_Solar_Decathlon_2007.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1998" data-file-width="3024" height="198" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Technische_Universit%C3%A4t_Darmstadt_-_Solar_Decathlon_2007.jpg/300px-Technische_Universit%C3%A4t_Darmstadt_-_Solar_Decathlon_2007.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Technische_Universit%C3%A4t_Darmstadt_-_Solar_Decathlon_2007.jpg/450px-Technische_Universit%C3%A4t_Darmstadt_-_Solar_Decathlon_2007.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Technische_Universit%C3%A4t_Darmstadt_-_Solar_Decathlon_2007.jpg/600px-Technische_Universit%C3%A4t_Darmstadt_-_Solar_Decathlon_2007.jpg 2x" width="300"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Technische_Universit%C3%A4t_Darmstadt_-_Solar_Decathlon_2007.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Darmstadt_University_of_Technology" title="Darmstadt University of Technology">
         Darmstadt University of Technology
        </a>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">
         Germany
        </a>
        won the 2007
        <a href="/wiki/Solar_Decathlon" title="Solar Decathlon">
         Solar Decathlon
        </a>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">
         Washington, D.C.
        </a>
        with this
        <a href="/wiki/Passive_house" title="Passive house">
         passive house
        </a>
        designed specifically for the humid and hot subtropical climate
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
         <a href="#cite_note-18">
          [18]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Technically, PSH is highly efficient. Direct-gain systems can utilize (i.e. convert into "useful" heat) 65-70% of the energy of solar radiation that strikes the aperture or collector.
     </p>
     <p>
      Passive solar fraction (PSF) is the percentage of the required heat load met by PSH and hence represents potential reduction in heating costs. RETScreen International has reported a PSF of 20-50%. Within the field of
      <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">
       sustainability
      </a>
      , energy conservation even of the order of 15% is considered substantial.
     </p>
     <p>
      Other sources report the following PSFs:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       5-25% for modest systems
      </li>
      <li>
       40% for "highly optimized" systems
      </li>
      <li>
       Up to 75% for "very intense" systems
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      In favorable climates such as the southwest United States, highly optimized systems can exceed 75% PSF.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      For more information see
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_air_heat" title="Solar air heat">
       Solar Air Heat
      </a>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Key_passive_solar_building_design_concepts">
       Key passive solar building design concepts
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_solar_building_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Key passive solar building design concepts">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      There are six primary passive solar energy configurations:
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_20-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-20">
        [20]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       direct
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_gain" title="Solar gain">
        solar gain
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       indirect solar gain
      </li>
      <li>
       isolated solar gain
      </li>
      <li>
       heat storage
      </li>
      <li>
       insulation and glazing
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Passive_cooling" title="Passive cooling">
        passive cooling
       </a>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Direct_solar_gain">
       Direct solar gain
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_solar_building_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Direct solar gain">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Direct gain attempts to control the amount of direct
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">
       solar radiation
      </a>
      reaching the living space. This direct solar gain is a critical part of passive solar house designation as it imparts to a direct gain.
     </p>
     <p>
      The cost effectiveness of these configurations are currently being investigated in great detail and are demonstrating promising results.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Indirect_solar_gain">
       Indirect solar gain
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_solar_building_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Indirect solar gain">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Indirect gain attempts to control solar radiation reaching an area adjacent but not part of the living space. Heat enters the building through windows and is captured and stored in
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_mass" title="Thermal mass">
       thermal mass
      </a>
      (e.g. water tank, masonry wall) and slowly transmitted indirectly to the building through
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conduction" title="Thermal conduction">
       conduction
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">
       convection
      </a>
      . Efficiency can suffer from slow response (thermal lag) and heat losses at night. Other issues include the cost of
      <a href="/wiki/Insulated_glazing" title="Insulated glazing">
       insulated glazing
      </a>
      and developing effective systems to redistribute heat throughout the living area.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Isolated_solar_gain">
       Isolated solar gain
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_solar_building_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Isolated solar gain">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Isolated gain involves utilizing solar energy to passively move heat from or to the living space using a fluid, such as water or air by natural convection or forced
      <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">
       convection
      </a>
      . Heat gain can occur through a sunspace,
      <a href="/wiki/Sunroom" title="Sunroom">
       solarium
      </a>
      or solar closet. These areas may also be employed usefully as a greenhouse or drying cabinet. An equator-side sun room may have its exterior windows higher than the windows between the sun room and the interior living space, to allow the low winter sun to penetrate to the cold side of adjacent rooms. Glass placement and overhangs prevent solar gain during the summer.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth_cooling_tubes" title="Earth cooling tubes">
       Earth cooling tubes
      </a>
      or other
      <a href="/wiki/Passive_cooling" title="Passive cooling">
       passive cooling
      </a>
      techniques can keep a solarium cool in the summer.
     </p>
     <p>
      Measures should be taken to reduce heat loss at night e.g. window coverings or movable window insulation.
     </p>
     <p>
      Examples:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Thermosiphon" title="Thermosiphon">
        Thermosiphon
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Barra_system" title="Barra system">
        Barra system
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Double_envelope_house" title="Double envelope house">
        Double envelope house
       </a>
      </li>
      <li>
       Thermal buffer zone
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_heating" title="Solar heating">
        Solar space heating
       </a>
       system
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_chimney" title="Solar chimney">
        Solar chimney
       </a>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Heat_storage">
       Heat storage
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_solar_building_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Heat storage">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The sun doesn't shine all the time. Heat storage, or
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_mass" title="Thermal mass">
       thermal mass
      </a>
      , keeps the building warm when the sun can't heat it.
     </p>
     <p>
      In diurnal solar houses, the storage is designed for one or a few days. The usual method is a custom-constructed thermal mass. This includes a
      <a href="/wiki/Trombe_wall" title="Trombe wall">
       Trombe wall
      </a>
      , a ventilated concrete floor, a cistern, water wall or roof pond.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        [23]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is also feasible to use the thermal mass of the earth itself, either as-is or by incorporation into the structure by banking or using rammed earth as a structural medium.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        [24]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In subarctic areas, or areas that have long terms without solar gain (e.g. weeks of freezing fog), purpose-built thermal mass is very expensive. Don Stephens pioneered an experimental technique to use the ground as thermal mass large enough for annualized heat storage. His designs run an isolated thermosiphon 3m under a house, and insulate the ground with a 6m waterproof skirt.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        [25]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Equator-facing_glass">
       Equator-facing glass
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_solar_building_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Equator-facing glass">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The requirement for vertical equator-facing glass is different from the other three sides of a building.
      <a href="/wiki/Insulated_glazing" title="Insulated glazing">
       Reflective window coatings
      </a>
      and multiple panes of glass can reduce useful solar gain. However, direct-gain systems are more dependent on
      <a href="/wiki/Insulated_glazing" title="Insulated glazing">
       double or triple glazing
      </a>
      to reduce heat loss. Indirect-gain and isolated-gain configurations may still be able to function effectively with only single-pane glazing. Nevertheless, the optimal cost-effective solution is both location and system dependent.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Roof-angle_glass_.2F_Skylights">
       Roof-angle glass / Skylights
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_solar_building_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Roof-angle glass / Skylights">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Skylights admit harsh direct overhead sunlight and glare
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        [27]
       </a>
      </sup>
      either horizontally (a flat roof) or pitched at the same angle as the roof slope. In some cases, horizontal skylights are used with reflectors to increase the intensity of solar radiation (and harsh glare), depending on the roof
      <a href="/wiki/Angle_of_incidence_(optics)" title="Angle of incidence (optics)">
       angle of incidence
      </a>
      . When the winter sun is low on the horizon, most solar radiation reflects off of roof angled glass ( the
      <a href="/wiki/Angle_of_incidence_(optics)" title="Angle of incidence (optics)">
       angle of incidence
      </a>
      is nearly parallel to roof-angled glass morning and afternoon ). When the summer sun is high, it is nearly perpendicular to roof-angled glass, which maximizes solar gain at the wrong time of year, and acts like a solar furnace. Skylights should be covered and well-insulated to reduce
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_convection" title="Natural convection">
       natural convection
      </a>
      ( warm air rising ) heat loss on cold winter nights, and intense solar heat gain during hot spring/summer/fall days.
     </p>
     <p>
      The equator-facing side of a building is south in the northern hemisphere, and north in the southern hemisphere. Skylights on roofs that face away from the equator provide mostly indirect illumination, except for summer days when the sun may rise on the non-equator side of the building (at some
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latitudes" title="Latitudes">
       latitudes
      </a>
      ). Skylights on east-facing roofs provide maximum direct light and solar heat gain in the summer morning. West-facing skylights provide afternoon sunlight and heat gain during the hottest part of the day.
     </p>
     <p>
      Some skylights have expensive glazing that partially reduces summer solar heat gain, while still allowing some visible light transmission. However, if visible light can pass through it, so can some radiant heat gain (they are both
      <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">
       electromagnetic radiation
      </a>
      waves).
     </p>
     <p>
      You can partially reduce some of the unwanted roof-angled-glazing summer solar heat gain by installing a skylight in the shade of
      <a href="/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">
       deciduous
      </a>
      (leaf-shedding) trees, or by adding a movable insulated opaque window covering on the inside or outside of the skylight. This would eliminate the daylight benefit in the summer. If tree limbs hang over a roof, they will increase problems with leaves in rain gutters, possibly cause roof-damaging
      <a href="/wiki/Ice_dam_(roof)" title="Ice dam (roof)">
       ice dams
      </a>
      , shorten roof life, and provide an easier path for pests to enter your attic. Leaves and twigs on skylights are unappealing, difficult to clean, and can increase the glazing breakage risk in wind storms.
     </p>
     <p>
      "Sawtooth roof glazing" with vertical-glass-only can bring some of the passive solar building design benefits into the core of a commercial or industrial building, without the need for any roof-angled glass or skylights.
     </p>
     <p>
      Skylights provide daylight. The only view they provide is essentially straight up in most applications. Well-insulated
      <a href="/wiki/Light_tube" title="Light tube">
       light tubes
      </a>
      can bring daylight into northern rooms, without using a skylight. A passive-solar greenhouse provides abundant daylight for the equator-side of the building.
     </p>
     <p>
      Infrared
      <a href="/wiki/Thermography" title="Thermography">
       thermography
      </a>
      color thermal imaging cameras ( used in formal
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_audit" title="Energy audit">
       energy audits
      </a>
      ) can quickly document the negative thermal impact of roof-angled glass or a skylight on a cold winter night or hot summer day.
     </p>
     <p>
      The U.S. Department of Energy states: "vertical glazing is the overall best option for sunspaces."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        [28]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Roof-angled glass and sidewall glass are not recommended for passive solar sunspaces.
     </p>
     <p>
      The U.S. DOE explains drawbacks to roof-angled glazing: Glass and plastic have little structural strength. When installed vertically, glass (or plastic) bears its own weight because only a small area (the top edge of the glazing) is subject to gravity. As the glass tilts off the vertical axis, however, an increased area (now the sloped cross-section) of the glazing has to bear the force of gravity. Glass is also brittle; it does not flex much before breaking. To counteract this, you usually must increase the thickness of the glazing or increase the number of structural supports to hold the glazing. Both increase overall cost, and the latter will reduce the amount of solar gain into the sunspace.
     </p>
     <p>
      Another common problem with sloped glazing is its increased exposure to the weather. It is difficult to maintain a good seal on roof-angled glass in intense sunlight. Hail, sleet, snow, and wind may cause material failure. For occupant safety, regulatory agencies usually require sloped glass to be made of safety glass, laminated, or a combination thereof, which reduce solar gain potential. Most of the roof-angled glass on the Crowne Plaza Hotel Orlando Airport sunspace was destroyed in a single windstorm. Roof-angled glass increases construction cost, and can increase insurance premiums. Vertical glass is less susceptible to weather damage than roof-angled glass.
     </p>
     <p>
      It is difficult to control solar heat gain in a sunspace with sloped glazing during the summer and even during the middle of a mild and sunny winter day. Skylights are the antithesis of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zero_energy_building" title="Zero energy building">
       zero energy building
      </a>
      Passive Solar Cooling in climates with an air conditioning requirement.
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Angle_of_incident_radiation">
       Angle of incident radiation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_solar_building_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Angle of incident radiation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The amount of solar gain transmitted through glass is also affected by the angle of the incident
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">
       solar radiation
      </a>
      .
      <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">
       Sunlight
      </a>
      striking glass within 20 degrees of
      <a href="/wiki/Perpendicular" title="Perpendicular">
       perpendicular
      </a>
      is mostly transmitted through the glass, whereas sunlight at more than 35 degrees from perpendicular is mostly
      <a href="/wiki/Reflection_(physics)" title="Reflection (physics)">
       reflected
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        [29]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      All of these factors can be modeled more precisely with a photographic
      <a href="/wiki/Light_meter" title="Light meter">
       light meter
      </a>
      and a
      <a href="/wiki/Heliodon" title="Heliodon">
       heliodon
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_bench" title="Optical bench">
       optical bench
      </a>
      , which can quantify the ratio of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reflectivity" title="Reflectivity">
       reflectivity
      </a>
      to
      <a href="/wiki/Transmittance" title="Transmittance">
       transmissivity
      </a>
      , based on
      <a href="/wiki/Angle_of_incidence_(optics)" title="Angle of incidence (optics)">
       angle of incidence
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Alternatively,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passive_solar" title="Passive solar">
       passive solar
      </a>
      computer software can determine the impact of
      <a href="/wiki/Sun_path" title="Sun path">
       sun path
      </a>
      , and cooling-and-heating
      <a href="/wiki/Degree_day" title="Degree day">
       degree days
      </a>
      on
      <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">
       energy
      </a>
      performance. Regional climatic conditions are often available from local weather services.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Operable_shading_and_insulation_devices">
       Operable shading and insulation devices
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_solar_building_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Operable shading and insulation devices">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      A design with too much equator-facing glass can result in excessive winter, spring, or fall day heating, uncomfortably bright living spaces at certain times of the year, and excessive heat transfer on winter nights and summer days.
     </p>
     <p>
      Although the sun is at the same altitude 6-weeks before and after the solstice, the heating and cooling requirements before and after the solstice are significantly different. Heat storage on the Earth's surface causes "thermal lag." Variable cloud cover influences solar gain potential. This means that latitude-specific fixed window overhangs, while important, are not a complete seasonal solar gain control solution.
     </p>
     <p>
      Control mechanisms (such as manual-or-motorized interior insulated drapes, shutters, exterior roll-down shade screens, or retractable awnings) can compensate for differences caused by thermal lag or cloud cover, and help control daily / hourly solar gain requirement variations.
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Home_automation" title="Home automation">
       Home automation
      </a>
      systems that monitor temperature, sunlight, time of day, and room occupancy can precisely control motorized window-shading-and-insulation devices.
     </p>

     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Passive_solar_lighting">
       Passive solar lighting
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_solar_building_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Passive solar lighting">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passive_solar_lighting" title="Passive solar lighting">
       Passive solar lighting
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passive_solar_lighting" title="Passive solar lighting">
       Passive solar lighting
      </a>
      techniques enhance taking advantage of
      <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">
       natural
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Daylighting" title="Daylighting">
       illumination
      </a>
      for interiors, and so reduce reliance on artificial lighting systems.
     </p>
     <p>
      This can be achieved by careful building design, orientation, and placement of window sections to collect light. Other creative solutions involve the use of reflecting surfaces to admit daylight into the interior of a building. Window sections should be adequately sized, and to avoid
      <a href="/wiki/Over-illumination" title="Over-illumination">
       over-illumination
      </a>
      can be shielded with a
      <a href="/wiki/Brise_soleil" title="Brise soleil">
       Brise soleil
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Awning" title="Awning">
       awnings
      </a>
      , well placed trees, glass coatings, and other passive and active devices.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_20-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-20">
        [20]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Another major issue for many
      <a href="/wiki/Window" title="Window">
       window
      </a>
      systems is that they can be potentially vulnerable sites of excessive thermal gain or heat loss. Whilst high mounted
      <a href="/wiki/Clerestory" title="Clerestory">
       clerestory
      </a>
      window and traditional
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skylight_(window)" title="Skylight (window)">
       skylights
      </a>
      can introduce daylight in poorly oriented sections of a building, unwanted heat transfer may be hard to control.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        [30]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        [31]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Thus, energy that is saved by reducing artificial lighting is often more than offset by the energy required for operating
      <a href="/wiki/HVAC" title="HVAC">
       HVAC
      </a>
      systems to maintain
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_comfort" title="Thermal comfort">
       thermal comfort
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Various methods can be employed to address this including but not limited to
      <a href="/wiki/Window_covering" title="Window covering">
       window coverings
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Insulated_glazing" title="Insulated glazing">
       insulated glazing
      </a>
      and novel materials such as
      <a href="/wiki/Aerogel" title="Aerogel">
       aerogel
      </a>
      semi-transparent insulation,
      <a href="/wiki/Optical_fiber" title="Optical fiber">
       optical fiber
      </a>
      embedded in walls or roof, or
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.ornl.gov/sci/solar/" rel="nofollow">
       hybrid solar lighting at Oak Ridge National Laboratory
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Reflecting elements, from active and
      <a href="/wiki/Passive_daylighting" title="Passive daylighting">
       passive daylighting
      </a>
      collectors, such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_shelves" title="Light shelves">
       light shelves
      </a>
      , lighter wall and floor colors,
      <a href="/wiki/Mirror" title="Mirror">
       mirrored
      </a>
      wall sections, interior walls with upper glass panels, and clear or translucent glassed hinged
      <a href="/wiki/Door" title="Door">
       doors
      </a>
      and
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sliding_glass_doors&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sliding glass doors (page does not exist)">
       sliding glass doors
      </a>
      take the captured light and passively reflect it further inside. The light can be from passive windows or skylights and solar
      <a href="/wiki/Light_tube" title="Light tube">
       light tubes
      </a>
      or from
      <a href="/wiki/Active_daylighting" title="Active daylighting">
       active daylighting
      </a>
      sources. In traditional
      <a href="/wiki/Japanese_architecture" title="Japanese architecture">
       Japanese architecture
      </a>
      the
      <a href="/wiki/Sh%C5%8Dji" title="Shji">
       Shji
      </a>
      sliding panel doors, with translucent
      <a href="/wiki/Washi" title="Washi">
       Washi
      </a>
      screens, are an original precedent.
      <a href="/wiki/International_Style_(architecture)" title="International Style (architecture)">
       International style
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Modern_architecture" title="Modern architecture">
       Modernist
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Mid-century_modern" title="Mid-century modern">
       Mid-century modern
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">
       architecture
      </a>
      were earlier innovators of this passive penetration and reflection in industrial, commercial, and residential applications.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Passive_solar_water_heating">
       Passive solar water heating
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_solar_building_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Passive solar water heating">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_hot_water" title="Solar hot water">
       Solar hot water
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      There are many ways to use
      <a href="/wiki/Solar_thermal_energy" title="Solar thermal energy">
       solar thermal energy
      </a>
      to heat water for domestic use. Different active-and-passive
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_hot_water" title="Solar hot water">
       solar hot water
      </a>
      technologies have different location-specific economic
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cost_benefit_analysis" title="Cost benefit analysis">
       cost benefit analysis
      </a>
      implications.
     </p>
     <p>
      Fundamental passive solar hot water heating involves no pumps or anything electrical. It is very cost effective in climates that do not have lengthy sub-freezing, or very-cloudy, weather conditions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        [32]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Other active solar water heating technologies, etc. may be more appropriate for some locations.
     </p>
     <p>
      It is possible to have active solar hot water which is also capable of being "off grid" and qualifies as sustainable. This is done by the use of a photovoltaic cell which uses energy from the sun to power the pumps.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2010)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_to_the_Passive_House_standard_in_Europe">
       Comparison to the Passive House standard in Europe
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_solar_building_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Comparison to the Passive House standard in Europe">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a href="/wiki/Passive_house" title="Passive house">
       Passive house
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      There is growing momentum in Europe for the approach espoused by the
      <a href="/wiki/Passive_house" title="Passive house">
       Passive House
      </a>
      (
      <i>
       Passivhaus
      </i>
      in German) Institute in Germany. Rather than relying solely on traditional passive solar design techniques, this approach seeks to make use of all passive sources of heat, minimises energy usage, and emphasises the need for high levels of insulation reinforced by meticulous attention to detail in order to address thermal bridging and cold air infiltration. Most of the buildings built to the Passive House standard also incorporate an active
      <a href="/wiki/Heat_recovery_ventilation" title="Heat recovery ventilation">
       heat recovery ventilation
      </a>
      unit with or without a small (typically 1kW) incorporated heating component.
     </p>
     <p>
      The energy design of Passive House buildings is developed using a spreadsheet-based modeling tool called the Passive House Planning Package (PHPP) which is updated periodically. The current version is PHPP2007, where 2007 is the year of issue. A building may be certified as a "Passive House" when it can be shown that it meets certain criteria, the most important being that the annual specific heat demand for the house should not exceed 15kWh/m
      <sup>
       2
      </sup>
      a.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Design_tools">
       Design tools
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_solar_building_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Design tools">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Traditionally a
      <a href="/wiki/Heliodon" title="Heliodon">
       heliodon
      </a>
      was used to simulate the altitude and azimuth of the sun shining on a model building at any time of any day of the year.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        [33]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In modern times, computer programs can model this phenomenon and integrate local climate data (including site impacts such as
      <a href="/wiki/Shadow" title="Shadow">
       overshadowing
      </a>
      and physical obstructions) to predict the solar gain potential for a particular building design over the course of a year.
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GPS" title="GPS">
       GPS
      </a>
      -based
      <a href="/wiki/Smartphone" title="Smartphone">
       smartphone
      </a>
      applications can now do this inexpensively on a hand held device. These
      <a href="/wiki/Design_tool" title="Design tool">
       design tools
      </a>
      provide the passive solar designer the ability to evaluate local conditions, design elements and orientation prior to construction. Energy performance optimization normally requires an iterative-refinement design-and-evaluate process. There is no such thing as a "one-size-fits-all" universal passive solar building design that would work well in all locations.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Levels_of_application">
       Levels of application
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_solar_building_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Levels of application">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Many detached suburban houses can achieve reductions in heating expense without obvious changes to their appearance, comfort or usability.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
       <a href="#cite_note-34">
        [34]
       </a>
      </sup>
      This is done using good siting and window positioning, small amounts of thermal mass, with good-but-conventional insulation, weatherization, and an occasional supplementary heat source, such as a central radiator connected to a (solar) water heater. Sunrays may fall on a wall during the daytime and raise the temperature of its
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_mass" title="Thermal mass">
       thermal mass
      </a>
      . This will then
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_radiation" title="Thermal radiation">
       radiate
      </a>
      heat into the building in the evening. External shading, or a radiant barrier plus air gap, may be used to reduce undesirable summer solar gain.
     </p>
     <p>
      An extension of the "passive solar" approach to seasonal solar capture and storage of heat and cooling. These designs attempt to capture warm-season solar heat, and convey it to a
      <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_thermal_energy_storage" title="Seasonal thermal energy storage">
       seasonal thermal store
      </a>
      for use months later during the cold season ("annualised passive solar.") Increased storage is achieved by employing large amounts of thermal mass or
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_coupling&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Earth coupling (page does not exist)">
       earth coupling
      </a>
      . Anecdotal reports suggest they can be effective but no formal study has been conducted to demonstrate their superiority. The approach also can move cooling into the warm season. Examples:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_Annual_Heat_Storage&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Passive Annual Heat Storage (page does not exist)">
        Passive Annual Heat Storage
       </a>
       (PAHS) - by John Hait
      </li>
      <li>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Annualized_Geothermal_Solar" title="Annualized Geothermal Solar">
        Annualized Geothermal Solar
       </a>
       (AGS) heating - by Don Stephen
      </li>
      <li>
       <a href="/wiki/Earth_sheltering" title="Earth sheltering">
        Earthed-roof
       </a>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      A "purely passive" solar-heated house would have no mechanical furnace unit, relying instead on energy captured from sunshine, only supplemented by "incidental" heat energy given off by lights, computers, and other task-specific appliances (such as those for cooking, entertainment, etc.), showering, people and pets. The use of natural convection air currents (rather than mechanical devices such as fans) to circulate air is related, though not strictly solar design. Passive solar building design sometimes uses limited electrical and mechanical controls to operate dampers, insulating shutters, shades, awnings, or reflectors. Some systems enlist small fans or solar-heated chimneys to improve convective air-flow. A reasonable way to analyse these systems is by measuring their
      <a href="/wiki/Coefficient_of_performance" title="Coefficient of performance">
       coefficient of performance
      </a>
      . A heat pump might use 1 J for every 4 J it delivers giving a COP of 4. A system that only uses a 30 W fan to more-evenly distribute 10kW of solar heat through an entire house would have a COP of 300.
     </p>
     <p>
      Passive solar building design is often a foundational element of a cost-effective
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zero_energy_building" title="Zero energy building">
       zero energy building
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        [35]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
       <a href="#cite_note-36">
        [36]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Although a ZEB uses multiple passive solar building design concepts, a ZEB is usually not purely passive, having active mechanical renewable energy generation systems such as:
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine" title="Wind turbine">
       wind turbine
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaics" title="Photovoltaics">
       photovoltaics
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Micro_hydro" title="Micro hydro">
       micro hydro
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geothermal" title="Geothermal">
       geothermal
      </a>
      , and other emerging alternative energy sources.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Passive_solar_design_on_skyscrapers">
       Passive solar design on skyscrapers
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Passive_solar_building_design&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Passive solar design on skyscrapers">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      There has been recent interest in the utilization of the large amounts of surface area on skyscrapers to improve their overall energy efficiency. Because skyscrapers are increasingly ubiquitous in urban environments, yet require large amounts of energy to operate, there is potential for large amounts of energy savings employing passive solar design techniques. One study,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
       <a href="#cite_note-37">
        [37]
       </a>
      </sup>
      which analyzed the proposed
      <a href="/wiki/22_Bishopsgate" title="22 Bishopsgate">
       22 Bishopsgate
      </a>
      tower in London, found that a 35% energy decrease in demand can theoretically be achieved through indirect solar gains, by rotating the building to achieve optimum ventilation and daylight penetration, usage of high thermal mass flooring material to decrease temperature fluctuation inside the building, and using double or triple glazed low emissivity window glass for direct solar gain. Indirect solar gain techniques included moderating wall heat flow by variations of wall thickness (from 20 to 30cm), using
      <a href="/wiki/Glazing_(window)" title="Glazing (window)">
       window glazing
      </a>
      on the outdoor space to prevent heat loss, dedicating 15-20% of floor area for thermal storage, and implementing a
      <a href="/wiki/Trombe_wall" title="Trombe wall">
       Trombe wall
      </a>
      to absorb heat entering the space. Overhangs are used to block direct sunlight in the summer, and allow it in the winter, and heat reflecting blinds are inserted between the thermal wall and the glazing to limit heat build-up in the summer months.
     </p>
     <p>
      Another study
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        [38]
       </a>
      </sup>
      analyzed double-green skin facade (DGSF) on the outside of high rise buildings in Hong Kong. Such a green facade, or vegetation covering the outer walls, can combat the usage of air conditioning greatly - as much as 80%, as discovered by the researchers.
     </p>
     <p>
      In more temperate climates, strategies such as glazing, adjustment of window-to-wall ratio, sun shading and roof strategies can offer considerable energy savings, in the 30% to 60% range.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
       <a href="#cite_note-39">
        [39]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
