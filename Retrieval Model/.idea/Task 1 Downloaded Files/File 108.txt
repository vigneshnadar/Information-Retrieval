     <p>
      The
      <b>
       Aguçadoura Wave Farm
      </b>
      was the world's first
      <a href="/wiki/Wave_farm" title="Wave farm">
       wave farm
      </a>
      . It was located 5 km (3 mi) offshore near
      <a href="/wiki/P%C3%B3voa_de_Varzim" title="Póvoa de Varzim">
       Póvoa de Varzim
      </a>
      north of
      <a href="/wiki/Porto" title="Porto">
       Porto
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">
       Portugal
      </a>
      . The farm was designed to use three
      <a href="/wiki/Pelamis_Wave_Energy_Converter" title="Pelamis Wave Energy Converter">
       Pelamis Wave Energy Converters
      </a>
      to convert the motion of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_surface_wave" title="Ocean surface wave">
       ocean surface waves
      </a>
      into electricity, totalling to 2.25
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megawatt" title="Megawatt">
       MW
      </a>
      in total installed capacity. The farm was officially opened on 23 September 2008, by the Portuguese Minister of Economy.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gov.pt_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-gov.pt-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The wave farm was shut down two months after the official opening in November 2008.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pelamis_Sinks_3-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Pelamis_Sinks-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEO_Exits_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-CEO_Exits-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Pelamis_machines">
       Pelamis machines
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Agu%C3%A7adoura_Wave_Farm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Pelamis machines">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pelamis_machine_installed_at_the_Agucadoura_Wave_Park.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1524" data-file-width="3371" height="99" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Pelamis_machine_installed_at_the_Agucadoura_Wave_Park.JPG/220px-Pelamis_machine_installed_at_the_Agucadoura_Wave_Park.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Pelamis_machine_installed_at_the_Agucadoura_Wave_Park.JPG/330px-Pelamis_machine_installed_at_the_Agucadoura_Wave_Park.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Pelamis_machine_installed_at_the_Agucadoura_Wave_Park.JPG/440px-Pelamis_machine_installed_at_the_Agucadoura_Wave_Park.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pelamis_machine_installed_at_the_Agucadoura_Wave_Park.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A Pelamis machine at the wave park.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Developed by the Scottish company
      <a href="/wiki/Pelamis_Wave_Power" title="Pelamis Wave Power">
       Pelamis Wave Power
      </a>
      , the Pelamis machine is made up of connected sections which flex and bend relative to one another as waves run along the structure. This motion is resisted by
      <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_ram" title="Hydraulic ram">
       hydraulic rams
      </a>
      which pump high pressure oil through
      <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_machinery" title="Hydraulic machinery">
       hydraulic motors
      </a>
      which in turn drive
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_generator" title="Electrical generator">
       electrical generators
      </a>
      . The three machines which made up the Aguçadoura Wave Park were each rated at a peak output of 750
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilowatt" title="Kilowatt">
       kW
      </a>
      , giving an installed peak capacity of 2.25 MW, enough to meet the average electricity demand of more than 1,500 Portuguese homes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The average output from a Pelamis machine will depend on the wave resource in a particular area. The higher the resource the higher the average output. According to information on the Pelamis web site, it appears that the average power output for a Pelamis wave machine is about 150 kW.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Average_Resource_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Average_Resource-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Project_management">
       Project management
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Agu%C3%A7adoura_Wave_Farm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Project management">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The project was originally conceived by the Portuguese renewable energy company
      <a href="/wiki/Enersis" title="Enersis">
       Enersis
      </a>
      , which developed and financed the project and which was subsequently bought by the Australian infrastructure company
      <a href="/wiki/Babcock_%26_Brown" title="Babcock &amp; Brown">
       Babcock &amp; Brown
      </a>
      in December 2005. In the last quarter of 2008, Babcock &amp; Brown had its shares suspended and has been in a managed process of selling its assets, including the Aguçadoura project. In March 2009, Babcock &amp; Brown went into voluntary administration.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In November 2008, the Pelamis machines were brought back to harbor at
      <a href="/wiki/Leix%C3%B5es" title="Leixões">
       Leixões
      </a>
      due to a technical problem with some of the bearings for which a solution has been found. However, the machines are likely to remain off-line until a new partner is found to take over Babcock &amp; Brown’s 77% share in the project. This seems unlikely, because according to Pelamis "those machines are sub-optimal" and the owner is trying to sell them.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEO_Exits_4-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-CEO_Exits-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Pelamis is now focusing its efforts on the new P2 machine,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      which has been tested in Orkney in Scotland since 2010.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
