     <p>
      A
      <b>
       tidal stream generator,
      </b>
      often referred to as a
      <b>
       tidal energy converter (TEC)
      </b>
      is a machine that extracts
      <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">
       energy
      </a>
      from moving masses of water, in particular
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tides" title="Tides">
       tides
      </a>
      , although the term is often used in reference to machines designed to extract energy from run of river or tidal estuarine sites. Certain types of these machines function very much like underwater
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine" title="Wind turbine">
       wind turbines
      </a>
      , and are thus often referred to as
      <b>
       tidal turbines
      </b>
      . They were first conceived in the 1970s during the oil crisis.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASAP._Web._8_October_2009_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-ASAP._Web._8_October_2009-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Tidal stream generators are the cheapest and the least ecologically damaging among the
      <a href="/wiki/Tidal_power#Generating_methods" title="Tidal power">
       three main forms
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Tidal_power" title="Tidal power">
       tidal power
      </a>
      generation.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Similarity_to_wind_turbines">
       Similarity to wind turbines
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tidal_stream_generator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Similarity to wind turbines">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Tidal stream generators draw energy from water currents in much the same way as
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine" title="Wind turbine">
       wind turbines
      </a>
      draw energy from air currents. However, the potential for power generation by an individual tidal turbine can be greater than that of similarly rated wind energy turbine. The higher density of water relative to air (water is about 800 times the density of air) means that a single generator can provide significant power at low tidal flow velocities compared with similar wind speed.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Given that power varies with the density of medium and the cube of velocity, water speeds of nearly one-tenth the speed of wind provide the same power for the same size of turbine system; however this limits the application in practice to places where tide speed is at least 2 knots (1 m/s) even close to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neap_tide" title="Neap tide">
       neap tides
      </a>
      . Furthermore, at higher speeds in a flow between 2 and 3 metres per second in seawater a tidal turbine can typically access four times as much energy per rotor swept area as a similarly rated power wind turbine.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_tidal_stream_generators">
       Types of tidal stream generators
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tidal_stream_generator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types of tidal stream generators">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      No standard tidal stream generator has emerged as the clear winner, among a large variety of designs. Several prototypes have shown promise with many companies making bold claims, some of which are yet to be independently verified, but they have not operated commercially for extended periods to establish performances and rates of return on investments.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/European_Marine_Energy_Centre" title="European Marine Energy Centre">
       European Marine Energy Centre
      </a>
      recognizes six principal types of tidal energy converter. They are horizontal axis turbines, vertical axis turbines, oscillating hydrofoils, venturi devices, Archimedes screws and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidal_kite" title="Tidal kite">
       tidal kites
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      These are close in concept to traditional windmills, but operating under the sea. They have most of the prototypes currently operating, including:
     </p>
     <p>
      Tocardo,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
      a Dutch-based company, has been running tidal turbines since 2008 on the Afsluitdijk, near Den Oever.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tocardo.com_6-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-tocardo.com-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.tocardo.com/siteAssets/assets/uploads/typical_day.png" rel="nofollow">
       Typical production data of tidal generator
      </a>
      shown of the T100 model as applied in Den Oever.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tocardo.com_6-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-tocardo.com-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Currently 1 River model (R1) and 2 tidal models (T) are in production with a 3rd T3 coming soon. Power production for the T1 is around 100 kW and around 200 kW for the T2.
     </p>

     <p>
      The AR-1000, a 1MW turbine developed by Atlantis Resources Corporation that was successfully deployed at the EMEC facility during the summer of 2011. The AR series are commercial scale, horizontal axis turbines designed for open ocean deployment. AR turbines feature a single rotor set with fixed pitch blades. The AR turbine is rotated as required with each tidal exchange. This is done in the slack period between tides and held in place for the optimal heading for the next tide. AR turbines are rated at 1MW @ 2.65 m/s of water flow velocity.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Kvalsund" title="Kvalsund">
       Kvalsund
      </a>
      installation is south of
      <a href="/wiki/Hammerfest" title="Hammerfest">
       Hammerfest
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_Norway" title="Energy in Norway">
       Norway
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Although still a prototype, a turbine with a reported capacity of 300 kW was connected to the grid on 13 November 2003.
     </p>
     <p>
      Seaflow, a 300 kW Periodflow marine current propeller type turbine was installed by
      <a href="/wiki/Marine_Current_Turbines" title="Marine Current Turbines">
       Marine Current Turbines
      </a>
      off the coast of
      <a href="/wiki/Lynmouth" title="Lynmouth">
       Lynmouth
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">

      </a>
      , England, in 2003.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The 11m diameter turbine generator was fitted to a steel pile which was driven into the seabed. As a prototype, it was connected to a dump load, not to the grid.
     </p>
     <p>
      In April 2007
      <a href="/wiki/Verdant_Power" title="Verdant Power">
       Verdant Power
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      began running a prototype project in the
      <a href="/wiki/East_River" title="East River">
       East River
      </a>
      between
      <a href="/wiki/Queens" title="Queens">
       Queens
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Roosevelt_Island" title="Roosevelt Island">
       Roosevelt Island
      </a>
      in New York City; it was the first major tidal-power project in the United States.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The strong currents pose challenges to the design: the blades of the 2006 and 2007 prototypes broke and new reinforced turbines were installed in September 2008.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Following the Seaflow trial, a full-size prototype, called
      <a href="/wiki/SeaGen" title="SeaGen">
       SeaGen
      </a>
      , was installed by Marine Current Turbines in Strangford Lough in Northern Ireland in April 2008. The turbine began to generate at full power of just over 1.2 MW in December 2008
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and is reported to have fed 150 kW into the grid for the first time on 17 July 2008, and has now contributed more than a gigawatt hour to consumers in Northern Ireland.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It is currently the only commercial scale device to have been installed anywhere in the world.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
      SeaGen is made up of two axial flow rotors, each of which drive a generator. The turbines are capable of generating electricity on both the ebb and flood tides because the rotor blades can pitch through 180˚.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
       <a href="#cite_note-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      OpenHydro,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
      an Irish company exploiting the Open-Centre Turbine developed in the U.S., has a prototype being tested at the
      <a href="/wiki/European_Marine_Energy_Centre" title="European Marine Energy Centre">
       European Marine Energy Centre
      </a>
      (EMEC), in Orkney, Scotland.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Evopod_lighter.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Evopod_lighter.jpg/220px-Evopod_lighter.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Evopod_lighter.jpg/330px-Evopod_lighter.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Evopod_lighter.jpg/440px-Evopod_lighter.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Evopod_lighter.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A 3D model of an Evopod tidal turbine
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      A prototype semi-submerged floating tethered tidal turbine called
      <a href="/wiki/Evopod" title="Evopod">
       Evopod
      </a>
      has been tested since June 2008
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        [18]
       </a>
      </sup>
      in Strangford Lough,
      <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">
       Northern Ireland
      </a>
      at 1/10 scale. The UK company developing it is called Ocean Flow Energy Ltd.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The advanced hull form maintains optimum heading into the tidal stream and is designed to operate in the peak flow of the water column.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2010, Tenax Energy of Australia proposed to put 450 turbines off the coast of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Darwin,_Australia" title="Darwin, Australia">
       Darwin, Australia
      </a>
      , in the
      <a href="/wiki/Clarence_Strait_(Northern_Territory)" title="Clarence Strait (Northern Territory)">
       Clarence Strait
      </a>
      . The turbines would feature a rotor section approximately 15 metres in diameter with a slightly larger gravity base. The turbines would operate in deep water well below shipping channels. Each turbine is forecast to produce energy for between 300 and 400 homes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        [20]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Tidalstream, a UK-based company, commissioned a scaled-down Triton 3 turbine in the Thames.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>
      It can be floated to its site, installed without cranes, jack-ups or divers and then ballasted into operating position. At full scale the Triton 3 in 30-50m deep water has a 3MW capacity, and the Triton 6 in 60-80m water has a capacity of up to 10MW, depending on the flow. Both platforms have man-access capability both in the operating position and in the float-out maintenance position.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Crossflow_turbines">
       Crossflow turbines
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tidal_stream_generator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Crossflow turbines">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Invented by
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georges_Darreius" title="Georges Darreius">
       Georges Darreius
      </a>
      in 1923 and patented in 1929, these turbines can be deployed either vertically or horizontally.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Gorlov_helical_turbine" title="Gorlov helical turbine">
       Gorlov turbine
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
       <a href="#cite_note-22">
        [22]
       </a>
      </sup>
      is a variant of the Darrieus design featuring a helical design that is in a large scale, commercial pilot in South Korea,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        [23]
       </a>
      </sup>
      starting with a 1MW plant that opened in May 2009
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
       <a href="#cite_note-24">
        [24]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and expanding to 90MW by 2013. Neptune Renewable Energy's Proteus project
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
       <a href="#cite_note-25">
        [25]
       </a>
      </sup>
      employs a shrouded vertical axis turbine that can be used to form an array in mainly estuarine conditions.
     </p>
     <p>
      In April 2008, the Ocean Renewable Power Company, LLC (ORPC) successfully completed testing its proprietary turbine-generator unit (TGU) prototype at ORPC's
      <a href="/wiki/Cobscook_Bay" title="Cobscook Bay">
       Cobscook Bay
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Western_Passage" title="Western Passage">
       Western Passage
      </a>
      tidal sites near
      <a href="/wiki/Eastport,_Maine" title="Eastport, Maine">
       Eastport, Maine
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
       <a href="#cite_note-26">
        [26]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The TGU is the core of the OCGen technology and utilizes advanced design cross-flow (ADCF) turbines to drive a permanent magnet generator located between the turbines and mounted on the same shaft. ORPC has developed TGU designs that can be used for generating power from river, tidal and deep water ocean currents.
     </p>
     <p>
      Trials in the
      <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Messina" title="Strait of Messina">
       Strait of Messina
      </a>
      , Italy, started in 2001 of the
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kobold_turbine&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kobold turbine (page does not exist)">
       Kobold turbine
      </a>
      concept.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
       <a href="#cite_note-27">
        [27]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Flow_augmented_turbines">
       Flow augmented turbines
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tidal_stream_generator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Flow augmented turbines">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sea_Bed_Turbine.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="363" data-file-width="667" height="120" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Sea_Bed_Turbine.png/220px-Sea_Bed_Turbine.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Sea_Bed_Turbine.png/330px-Sea_Bed_Turbine.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Sea_Bed_Turbine.png/440px-Sea_Bed_Turbine.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sea_Bed_Turbine.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A shrouded turbine
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Using flow augmentation measures, for example a duct or shroud, the incident power available to a turbine can be increased. The most common example uses a
      <a href="/wiki/Shrouded_tidal_turbine" title="Shrouded tidal turbine">
       shroud
      </a>
      to increase the flow rate through the turbine, which can be either axial or crossflow.
     </p>
     <p>
      The Australian company Tidal Energy Pty Ltd undertook successful commercial trials of efficient
      <a href="/wiki/Shrouded_tidal_turbine" title="Shrouded tidal turbine">
       shrouded tidal turbines
      </a>
      on the
      <a href="/wiki/Gold_Coast,_Queensland" title="Gold Coast, Queensland">
       Gold Coast, Queensland
      </a>
      in 2002. Tidal Energy delivered their shrouded turbine in northern Australia where some of the fastest recorded flows (11 m/s, 21 knots) are found. Two small turbines will provide 3.5 MW. Another larger 5 meter diameter turbine, capable of 800 kW in 4 m/s of flow, was planned as a tidal powered desalination showcase near Brisbane Australia.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Oscillating_devices">
       Oscillating devices
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tidal_stream_generator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Oscillating devices">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Oscillating devices do not have a rotating component, instead making use of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerofoil" title="Aerofoil">
       aerofoil
      </a>
      sections which are pushed sideways by the flow. Oscillating stream power extraction was proven with the omni- or bi-directional Wing'd Pump windmill.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        [28]
       </a>
      </sup>
      During 2003 a 150 kW oscillating hydroplane device, the Stingray, was tested off the Scottish coast.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
       <a href="#cite_note-29">
        [29]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The Stingray uses hydrofoils to create oscillation, which allows it to create hydraulic power. This hydraulic power is then used to power a hydraulic motor, which then turns a generator.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASAP._Web._8_October_2009_1-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-ASAP._Web._8_October_2009-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Pulse Tidal operate an oscillating hydrofoil device in the Humber estuary.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        [30]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Having secured funding from the EU, they are developing a commercial scale device to be commissioned 2012.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
       <a href="#cite_note-31">
        [31]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The bioSTREAM tidal power conversion system, uses the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biomimicry" title="Biomimicry">
       biomimicry
      </a>
      of swimming species, such as shark, tuna, and mackerel using their highly efficient
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thunniform" title="Thunniform">
       Thunniform
      </a>
      mode propulsion. It is produced by Australian company BioPower Systems.
     </p>
     <p>
      A 2 kW prototype relying on the use of two oscillating hydrofoils in a tandem configuration has been developed at Laval University and tested successfully near Quebec City, Canada, in 2009. A hydrodynamic efficiency of 40% has been achieved during the field tests.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
       <a href="#cite_note-32">
        [32]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Venturi_effect">
       Venturi effect
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tidal_stream_generator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Venturi effect">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      See also:
      <a href="/wiki/Venturi_effect" title="Venturi effect">
       Venturi effect
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      Venturi effect devices use a shroud or duct in order to generate a pressure differential which is used to run a secondary hydraulic circuit which is used to generate power. A device, the Hydro Venturi, is to be tested in San Francisco Bay.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
       <a href="#cite_note-33">
        [33]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Tidal_kite_turbines">
       Tidal kite turbines
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tidal_stream_generator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Tidal kite turbines">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      A
      <b>
       tidal kite turbine
      </b>
      is an underwater kite system or
      <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Paravane" title="Paravane">
       paravane
      </a>
      that converts
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidal_energy" title="Tidal energy">
       tidal energy
      </a>
      into electricity by moving through the tidal stream. An estimated 1% of 2011's global energy requirements could be provided by such devices at scale.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g1103_34-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-g1103-34">
        [34]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       History
      </dt>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Ernst Souczek of Vienna, Austria, on August 6, 1947, filed for a patent
      <a class="external text" href="https://www.google.com/patents/US2501696" rel="nofollow">
       US2501696
      </a>
      ; assignor of one-half to Wolfgang Kmentt, also of Vienna. Their water kite turbine disclosure demonstrated a rich art in water-kite turbines. In similar technology, many others prior to 2006 advanced water-kite and paravane electric generating systems. In 2006, a tidal kite turbine was developed by Swedish company Minesto. They conducted its first sea trial in Strangford Lough in Northern Ireland in the summer of 2011. The test used kites with wingspan of 1.4m.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g1103_34-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-g1103-34">
        [34]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 2013 the Deep Green pilot plant began operation off Northern Ireland. The plant uses
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_fiber" title="Carbon fiber">
       carbon fiber
      </a>
      kites with a wingspan of 8m (or 12m
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
       <a href="#cite_note-35">
        [35]
       </a>
      </sup>
      ). Each kite has a rated power of 120 kilowatts at a tidal flow of 1.3 meters per second.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iee1311_36-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-iee1311-36">
        [36]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Design
      </dt>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Minesto's kite has a wingspan of 8–14 metres (26–46 ft). The kite has neutral buoyancy, so doesn't sink as the tide turns from ebb to flow. Each kite is equipped with a gearless
      <a href="/wiki/Turbine" title="Turbine">
       turbine
      </a>
      to generate which is transmitted by the attachment cable to a transformer and then to the electricity grid. The turbine mouth is protected to protect marine life.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g1103_34-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-g1103-34">
        [34]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The 14-meter version has a rated power of 850 kilowatts at 1.7 meters per second.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iee1311_36-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-iee1311-36">
        [36]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dt>
       Operation
      </dt>
     </dl>
     <p>
      The kite is tethered by a cable to a fixed point. It "flies" through the current carrying a turbine. It moves in a
      <a href="/wiki/Figure-eight_loop" title="Figure-eight loop">
       figure-eight loop
      </a>
      to increase the speed of the water flowing through the turbine tenfold.
      <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">
       Force
      </a>
      increases with the cube of
      <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">
       velocity
      </a>
      , offering the potential to generate 1,000-fold more energy than a stationary generator.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g1103_34-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-g1103-34">
        [34]
       </a>
      </sup>
      That maneuver means the kite can operate in tidal streams that move too slowly to drive earlier tidal devices, such as the
      <a href="/wiki/SeaGen" title="SeaGen">
       SeaGen
      </a>
      turbine.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g1103_34-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-g1103-34">
        [34]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The kite was expected to work in flows as low 1–2.5 metres (3 ft 3 in–8 ft 2 in) per second, while first-generation devices need over 2.5s. Each kite will have a capacity to generate between 150 and 800 kW. They can be deployed in waters 50–300 metres (160–980 ft) deep.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g1103_34-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-g1103-34">
        [34]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Tidal_stream_testing">
       Tidal stream testing
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tidal_stream_generator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Tidal stream testing">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The world's first marine energy test facility was established in 2003 to kick start the development of the wave and tidal energy industry in the UK. Based in Orkney, Scotland, the
      <a class="external text" href="http://www.emec.org.uk/" rel="nofollow">
       European Marine Energy Centre (EMEC)
      </a>
      has supported the deployment of more wave and tidal energy devices than at any other single site in the world. EMEC provides a variety of test sites in real sea conditions. It's grid connected tidal test site is located at the Fall of Warness, off the island of Eday, in a narrow channel which concentrates the tide as it flows between the Atlantic Ocean and North Sea. This area has a very strong tidal current, which can travel up to 4 m/s (8 knots) in spring tides. Tidal energy developers currently testing at the site include Alstom (formerly Tidal Generation Ltd), ANDRITZ HYDRO Hammerfest, OpenHydro, Scotrenewables Tidal Power, and Voith.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
       <a href="#cite_note-38">
        [38]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

      <span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_plans">
       Commercial plans
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tidal_stream_generator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Commercial plans">

       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/RWE" title="RWE">
       RWE
      </a>
      's
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Npower_(UK)" title="Npower (UK)">
       npower
      </a>
      announced that it is in partnership with Marine Current Turbines to build a tidal farm of SeaGen turbines off the coast of
      <a href="/wiki/Anglesey" title="Anglesey">
       Anglesey
      </a>
      in Wales,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
       <a href="#cite_note-39">
        [39]
       </a>
      </sup>
      near
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Skerries,_Anglesey" title="The Skerries, Anglesey">

      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">

     <p>
      "The Skerries project located in Anglesey, Wales, will be one of the first arrays deployed using the Siemens owned Marine Current Turbines SeaGen S tidal turbines. The marine consent for the project was recently awarded, the first tidal array to be consented in Wales. The 10MW array will be fully operational in 2015." - CEO of Siemens Energy Hydro &amp; Ocean Unit Achim Wörner
     </p>
     <p>
      In November 2007, British company Lunar Energy announced that, in conjunction with
      <a href="/wiki/E.ON" title="E.ON">

     <p>
      Modern advances in
      <a href="/wiki/Turbine" title="Turbine">
       turbine
      </a>
      technology may eventually see large amounts of power generated from the ocean, especially tidal currents using the tidal stream designs but also from the major thermal current systems such as the
      <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">
       Gulf Stream
      </a>
      , which is covered by the more general term
      <a href="/wiki/Marine_current_power" title="Marine current power">
       marine current power
      </a>
      . Tidal stream turbines may be arrayed in high-velocity areas where natural tidal current flows are concentrated such as the west and east coasts of Canada, the
      <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Gibraltar" title="Strait of Gibraltar">
       Strait of Gibraltar
      </a>
      , the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bosporus" title="Bosporus">
       Bosporus
      </a>
      , and numerous sites in
      <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">
       Southeast Asia
      </a>
      and Australia. Such flows occur almost anywhere where there are entrances to bays and rivers, or between land masses where water currents are concentrated.
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_impacts">
       Environmental impacts
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tidal_stream_generator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Environmental impacts">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The main environmental concern with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidal_energy" title="Tidal energy">
       tidal energy
      </a>
      is associated with blade strike and entanglement of marine organisms as high speed water increases the risk of organisms being pushed near or through these devices. As with all offshore renewable energies, there is also a concern about how the creation of EMF and acoustic outputs may affect marine organisms. It should be noted that because these devices are in the water, the acoustic output can be greater than those created with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Offshore_wind_energy" title="Offshore wind energy">
       offshore wind energy
      </a>
      . Depending on the frequency and amplitude of sound generated by the tidal energy devices, this acoustic output can have varying effects on marine mammals (particularly those who echolocate to communicate and navigate in the marine environment such as
      <a href="/wiki/Dolphin" title="Dolphin">
       dolphins
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Whale" title="Whale">
       whales
      </a>
      ). Tidal energy removal can also cause environmental concerns such as degrading farfield water quality and disrupting sediment processes. Depending on the size of the project, these effects can range from small traces of sediment build up near the tidal device to severely affecting nearshore ecosystems and processes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62">
       <a href="#cite_note-62">
        [62]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      One study of the Roosevelt Island Tidal Energy (RITE, Verdant Power) project in the East River (New York City), utilized 24 split beam hydroacoustic sensors (
      <a href="/wiki/Scientific_echosounder" title="Scientific echosounder">
       scientific echosounder
      </a>
      ) to detect and track the movement of fish both upstream and downstream of each of six turbines. The results suggested (1) very few fish using this portion of the river, (2) those fish which did use this area were not using the portion of the river which would subject them to blade strikes, and (3) no evidence of fish traveling through blade areas.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2010)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Work is currently being conducted by the Northwest National Marine Renewable Energy Center (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NNMREC" title="NNMREC">
       NNMREC
      </a>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63">
       <a href="#cite_note-63">
        [63]
       </a>
      </sup>
      )to explore and establish tools and protocols for assessment of physical and biological conditions and monitor environmental changes associated with tidal energy development.
     </p>
