     <p>
      <i>
       <b>
        Greenhouse Solutions with Sustainable Energy
       </b>
      </i>
      is a 2007 book by Australian academic
      <a href="/wiki/Mark_Diesendorf" title="Mark Diesendorf">
       Mark Diesendorf
      </a>
      . The book puts forward a set of policies and strategies for implementing the most promising
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clean_energy" title="Clean energy">
       clean energy
      </a>
      technologies by all spheres of government, business and community organisations.
      <i>
       Greenhouse Solutions with Sustainable Energy
      </i>
      suggests that a mix of
      <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">
       efficient energy use
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">
       renewable energy
      </a>
      sources and
      <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">
       natural gas
      </a>
      (as a transitional fuel) offers a clean and feasible energy future for Australia.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unsw_1-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-unsw-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_themes">
       Structure and themes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenhouse_Solutions_with_Sustainable_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure and themes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The book is a comprehensive guide to sustainable energy systems
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-on_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-on-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and is structured in three sections:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Introduction to the basic concepts and latest scientific evidence regarding global warming.
      </li>
      <li>
       Assessment of energy technologies, including coal, nuclear and more sustainable alternatives.
      </li>
      <li>
       Discussion of policies and strategies needed to overcome the non-technical barriers to renewable energies and energy efficiency.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      Diesendorf argues that:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Ecologically sustainable energy technologies based on energy efficiency, renewable energy and natural gas are commercially available today, and that their implementation could halve Australia’s
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_emissions" title="Greenhouse gas emissions">
        greenhouse gas emissions
       </a>
       within a few decades.
      </li>
      <li>
       To implement these technologies, new policies must be developed and implemented by all three levels of government.
      </li>
      <li>
       The main barriers are neither technical nor economic, but rather our social institutions and the political power of the big greenhouse gas emitting industries: coal, oil, aluminium, cement and motor vehicles.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unsw_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-unsw-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Wind_power_variability">
       Wind power variability
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenhouse_Solutions_with_Sustainable_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Wind power variability">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Early in his career, Mark Diesendorf was a principal research scientist with
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CSIRO" title="CSIRO">
       CSIRO
      </a>
      where he was involved in early research on integrating
      <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">
       wind power
      </a>
      into electricity grids. This issue is discussed in some detail in
      <i>
       Greenhouse Solutions with Sustainable Energy
      </i>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Diesendorf explains that large-scale wind power is not "intermittent", because it does not start up or switch off instantaneously. In practice, the variations in thousands of wind turbines, spread out over several different sites and wind regimes, are smoothed. As the distance between sites increases, the correlation between wind speeds measured at those sites, decreases. This has been confirmed recently by studies conducted by
      <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Graham_Sinden&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Graham Sinden (page does not exist)">
       Graham Sinden
      </a>
      from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxford_University" title="Oxford University">
       Oxford University
      </a>
      :
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nine_4-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-nine-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <blockquote>
      <p>
       [Graham Sinden] analysed over 30 years of hourly wind speed data from 66 sites spread out over the United Kingdom. He found that the correlation coefficient of wind power fell from 0.6 at 200 km to 0.25 at 600 km separation (a perfect correlation would have a coefficient equal to 1.0.) There were no hours in the data set where wind speed was below the cut-in wind speed of a modern wind turbine throughout the United Kingdom, and low wind speed events affecting more than 90 per cent of the United Kingdom had an average recurrent rate of only one hour per year.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nine_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-nine-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
     </blockquote>
     <p>
      Diesendorf goes on to say that every conventional power station breaks down unexpectedly from time to time, causing an immediate loss of all its power. That is true intermittency, according to Diesendorf, and it is a particular type of variability that switches between full power and no power. Once a conventional power station has broken down, it may be offline for weeks, much longer than windless periods.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
       <a href="#cite_note-5">
        [5]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Quotes">
       Quotes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenhouse_Solutions_with_Sustainable_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Quotes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <ul>
      <li>
       "The enhanced greenhouse effect is arguably the most dangerous environmental problem and the most difficult political issue to be faced by the world in the 21st century." (p. 1)
      </li>
      <li>
       "The recent push for a revival of nuclear energy has been based on its claimed reduction in CO
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       emissions where it substitutes for coal-fired power stations. In reality, only reactor operation is CO
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       -free. All other stages of the nuclear fuel chain -- mining, milling, fuel fabrication, enrichment, reactor construction, decommissioning, and waste management -- use fossil fuels and hence emit CO
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       ..." (p. 252)
      </li>
      <li>
       "Global wind-power capacity continues to expand and, apart from the blip in 2006, its costs continue to decline steadily. Wind power is one of the few energy supply technologies that are ready for wide dissemination today, unlike coal with CO
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       capture and sequestration and unlike nuclear power. Wind can deliver deep cuts in CO
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       , while providing a hedge against fluctuating fossil fuel prices and reducing energy import dependence." (p. 126)
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">
       Critical reception
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenhouse_Solutions_with_Sustainable_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Critical reception">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Dick Nichols, in
      <i>
       Green Left
      </i>
      , states that
      <i>
       Greenhouse Solutions with Sustainable Energy
      </i>
      brings together much useful material about global warming and possible solutions:
     </p>
     <dl>
      <dd>
       Diesendorf's book concentrates into one volume a succinct analysis of global warming, a rebuttal of climate change scepticism, a thorough summary of the state of development of each renewable energy technology, a masterly demolition of false “solutions” to greenhouse (like
       <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">
        carbon sequestration
       </a>
       and nuclear power) and a presentation of strategies and policies for uprooting carbon-intensive power production in Australia. Add in chapters on saving energy and transport and urban redesign and
       <i>
        Greenhouse Solutions with Sustainable Energy
       </i>
       illuminates the reader about all the main features of the many-sided debate on how to make sustainable energy production the heart of the attack on climate change.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </dd>
     </dl>
     <p>
      Nichols suggests that if there’s one serious shortcoming in
      <i>
       Greenhouse Solutions with Sustainable Energy
      </i>
      it’s "a failure to consistently trace what rate of implementation of energy efficiency and uptake of renewable energy sources is necessary to confront the greenhouse emergency".
     </p>
     <p>
      Patrick O'Neill, in
      <i>
       Chain Reaction
      </i>
      , suggests that
      <i>
       Greenhouse Solutions with Sustainable Energy
      </i>
      is a comprehensive guide to sustainable energy systems, and that the book is "simply a joy to read". O'Neill explains that whilst an immense amount of technical and scientific detail is presented, the language is simple and the book is well laid out. It also covers often ignored areas of the sustainable energy discussion such as population issues, morality, social justice and equity. In conclusion, O'Neill states that the book is a "wonderfully energising piece of sedition", which calls for a "coordinated national strategy for non-violent action".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-on_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-on-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      George Wilkenfield, in
      <i>
       Australian Review of Public Affairs
      </i>
      , is more critical and suggests the book feels "strangely anachronistic", partly because it treats climate change as an issue that can be resolved through grassroots activism or by the opposition of protest movements to unjust regimes. Wilkenfield explains that "this rather misses the point that the continuing rise in global greenhouse gas emissions is not a byproduct of injustice or repression but of economic freedom in the West and the unrelenting economic growth by which repressive regimes such as China’s buy legitimacy. In general, very few people want to be freed from consumption—most want the freedom to consume even more". Wilkenfield goes on to say that Diesendorf misses some of the dynamics of consumption: energy use gives people comfort, speed, privacy and convenience. Many people actually enjoy driving and like their cars, and would continue to use them as long as they could afford to, even if public transport were available.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-george_7-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-george-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Nevertheless, in the end, Wilkenfield concedes that
      <i>
       Greenhouse Solutions with Sustainable Energy
      </i>
      is very suitable as a textbook, or as a handbook for activists, journalists or the more committed reader who is not afraid of numbers.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-george_7-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-george-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Author">
       Author
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenhouse_Solutions_with_Sustainable_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Author">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Mark Diesendorf teaches and researches
      <a href="/wiki/Ecologically_sustainable_development" title="Ecologically sustainable development">
       ecologically sustainable development
      </a>
      and greenhouse solutions at the Institute of Environmental Studies at the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_NSW" title="University of NSW">
       University of NSW
      </a>
      . Previously he has been a Principle Research Scientist at
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CSIRO" title="CSIRO">
       CSIRO
      </a>
      , Professor of Environmental Science at UTS and Vice-President of the Australia New Zealand Society for Ecological Economics.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
