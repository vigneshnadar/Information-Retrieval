      <b>
       Municipal solid
       <a href="/wiki/Waste" title="Waste">
        waste
       </a>
      </b>
      (
      <b>
       MSW
      </b>
      ), commonly known as
      <b>
       trash
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       garbage
      </b>
      in the
      <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">
       United States
      </a>
      and as
      <b>
       refuse
      </b>
      or
      <b>
       rubbish
      </b>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">
       Britain
      </a>
      , is a
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_waste_types" title="List of waste types">
       waste type
      </a>
      consisting of everyday items that are discarded by the public. "Garbage" can also refer specifically to
      <a href="/wiki/Food_waste" title="Food waste">
       food waste
      </a>
      , as in a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Garbage_disposal" title="Garbage disposal">
       garbage disposal
      </a>
      ; the two are sometimes collected separately.

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">
       Composition
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Municipal_solid_waste&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Composition">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The composition of municipal solid waste varies greatly from municipality to municipality (country to country) and changes significantly with time. In municipalities (countries) which have a well developed waste recycling system, the waste stream consists mainly of intractable wastes such as plastic film, and unrecyclable packaging materials. At the start of the 20th century, the majority of domestic waste (53%) in the UK consisted of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal_ash" title="Coal ash">
       coal ash
      </a>
      from open fires.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In developed municipalities (countries) without significant recycling activity it predominantly includes food wastes, market wastes, yard wastes, plastic containers and product packaging materials, and other miscellaneous solid wastes from residential, commercial, institutional, and industrial sources.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Most definitions of municipal solid waste do not include
      <a href="/wiki/Industrial_waste" title="Industrial waste">
       industrial wastes
      </a>
      , agricultural wastes,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medical_waste" title="Medical waste">
       medical waste
      </a>
      , radioactive waste or
      <a href="/wiki/Sewage_sludge" title="Sewage sludge">
       sewage sludge
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Waste collection is performed by the
      <a href="/wiki/Municipality" title="Municipality">
       municipality
      </a>
      within a given area. The term
      <i>
       residual waste
      </i>
      relates to waste left from household sources containing materials that have not been separated out or sent for reprocessing.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Components_of_solid_waste_management">
       Components of solid waste management
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Municipal_solid_waste&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Components of solid waste management">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tri_des_d%C3%A9chets_(cropped).jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1637" data-file-width="2313" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Tri_des_d%C3%A9chets_%28cropped%29.jpg/220px-Tri_des_d%C3%A9chets_%28cropped%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Tri_des_d%C3%A9chets_%28cropped%29.jpg/330px-Tri_des_d%C3%A9chets_%28cropped%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Tri_des_d%C3%A9chets_%28cropped%29.jpg/440px-Tri_des_d%C3%A9chets_%28cropped%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tri_des_d%C3%A9chets_(cropped).jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Bins to collect
        <a href="/wiki/Paper_recycling" title="Paper recycling">
         paper
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_recycling" title="Aluminium recycling">
         aluminium
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Glass_recycling" title="Glass recycling">
         glass
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/PET_bottle_recycling" title="PET bottle recycling">
         PET bottles
        </a>
        and incinerable waste.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The municipal solid waste industry has four components:
      <a href="/wiki/Recycling" title="Recycling">
       recycling
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Compost" title="Compost">
       composting
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">
       disposal
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Waste-to-energy" title="Waste-to-energy">
       waste-to-energy
      </a>
      via incineration.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
       <a href="#cite_note-6">
        [6]
       </a>
      </sup>
      There is no single approach that can be applied to the management of all waste streams, therefore the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="Environmental Protection Agency">
       Environmental Protection Agency
      </a>
      , federal agency of the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_of_America" title="United States of America">
       United States of America
      </a>
      , developed a hierarchy ranking strategy for municipal solid waste.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
       <a href="#cite_note-7">
        [7]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Waste_hierarchy" title="Waste hierarchy">
       Waste Management Hierarchy
      </a>
      is made up of four levels ordered from most preferred to least preferred methods based on their environmental soundness: Source reduction and reuse; recycling or composting; energy recovery; treatment and disposal.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Collection">
       Collection
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Municipal_solid_waste&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Collection">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The functional element of collection includes not only the gathering of solid waste and recyclable materials, but also the transport of these materials, after collection, to the location where the collection vehicle is emptied. This location may be a materials processing facility, a transfer station or a landfill disposal site.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Waste_handling_and_separation.2C_storage_and_processing_at_the_source">
       Waste handling and separation, storage and processing at the source
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Municipal_solid_waste&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Waste handling and separation, storage and processing at the source">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Waste handling and separation involves activities associated with waste management until the waste is placed in storage containers for collection. Handling also encompasses the movement of loaded containers to the point of collection. Separating different types of waste components is an important step in the handling and storage of solid waste at the source.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Segregation_and_processing_and_transformation_of_solid_wastes">
       Segregation and processing and transformation of solid wastes
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Municipal_solid_waste&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Segregation and processing and transformation of solid wastes">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The types of means and facilities that are now used for the recovery of waste materials that have been separated at the source include curbside ('kerbside' in the UK) collection, drop-off and buy-back centers. The separation and processing of wastes that have been separated at the source and the separation of commingled wastes usually occur at a materials recovery facility, transfer stations, combustion facilities and disposal sites.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Transfer_and_transport">
       Transfer and transport
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Municipal_solid_waste&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Transfer and transport">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      This element involves two main steps. First, the waste is transferred from a smaller collection vehicle to larger transport equipment. The waste is then transported, usually over long distances, to a processing or disposal site.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Disposal">
       Disposal
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Municipal_solid_waste&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Disposal">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mixed_municipal_waste.JPG">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="280" data-file-width="422" height="133" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Mixed_municipal_waste.JPG/200px-Mixed_municipal_waste.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Mixed_municipal_waste.JPG/300px-Mixed_municipal_waste.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Mixed_municipal_waste.JPG/400px-Mixed_municipal_waste.JPG 2x" width="200"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mixed_municipal_waste.JPG" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Mixed municipal waste,
        <a href="/wiki/Hiriya" title="Hiriya">
         Hiriya
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Tel_Aviv" title="Tel Aviv">
         Tel Aviv
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Today, the disposal of wastes by land filling or land spreading is the ultimate fate of all solid wastes, whether they are residential wastes collected and transported directly to a landfill site, residual materials from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Materials_recovery_facilities" title="Materials recovery facilities">
       materials recovery facilities
      </a>
      (MRFs), residue from the combustion of solid waste,
      <a href="/wiki/Compost" title="Compost">
       compost
      </a>
      , or other substances from various solid waste processing facilities. A modern sanitary landfill is not a dump; it is an engineered facility used for disposing of solid wastes on land without creating nuisances or hazards to public health or safety, such as the problems of insects and the contamination of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ground_water" title="Ground water">
       ground water
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Reusing">
       Reusing
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Municipal_solid_waste&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Reusing">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In the recent years environmental organizations, such as
      <a href="/wiki/Freegle" title="Freegle">
       Freegle
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freecycle_Network" title="Freecycle Network">
       Freecycle Network
      </a>
      , have been gaining popularity for their online reuse networks. These networks provide a worldwide online registry of unwanted items that would otherwise be thrown away, for individuals and nonprofits to reuse or recycle. Therefore, this free Internet-based service reduces landfill pollution and promotes the
      <a href="/wiki/Gift_economy" title="Gift economy">
       gift economy
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Landfills">
       Landfills
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Municipal_solid_waste&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Landfills">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Landfills are created by land dumping. Land dumping methods vary, most commonly it involves the mass dumping of waste into a designated area, usually a hole or sidehill. After the waste is dumped, it is then compacted by large machines. When the dumping cell is full, it is then "sealed" with a plastic sheet and covered in several feet of dirt. This is the primary method of dumping in the United States because of the low cost and abundance of unused land in North America. Landfills pose the threat of pollution, and can intoxicate ground water. The signs of pollution are effectively masked by disposal companies and it is often hard to see any evidence. Usually landfills are surrounded by large walls or fences hiding the mounds of debris. Large amounts of chemical odor eliminating agent are sprayed in the air surrounding landfills to hide the evidence of the rotting waste inside the plant.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
       <a href="#cite_note-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_generation">
       Energy generation
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Municipal_solid_waste&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Energy generation">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Municipal solid waste can be used to generate energy. Several technologies have been developed that make the processing of MSW for energy generation cleaner and more economical than ever before, including landfill gas capture, combustion,
      <a href="/wiki/Pyrolysis" title="Pyrolysis">
       pyrolysis
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Gasification" title="Gasification">
       gasification
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plasma_arc_gasification" title="Plasma arc gasification">
       plasma arc
       <i>
        gasification
       </i>
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
      While older waste incineration plants emitted high levels of pollutants, recent regulatory changes and new technologies have significantly reduced this concern.
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">
       United States Environmental Protection Agency
      </a>
      (EPA) regulations in 1995 and 2000 under the
      <a href="/wiki/Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)" title="Clean Air Act (United States)">
       Clean Air Act
      </a>
      have succeeded in reducing emissions of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dioxins" title="Dioxins">
       dioxins
      </a>
      from
      <a href="/wiki/Waste-to-energy" title="Waste-to-energy">
       waste-to-energy
      </a>
      facilities by more than 99 percent below 1990 levels, while mercury emissions have been reduced by over 90 percent.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The EPA noted these improvements in 2003, citing waste-to-energy as a power source "with less environmental impact than almost any other source of
      <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">
       electricity
      </a>
      ".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
