     <p>
      <b>
       Incineration
      </b>
      is a
      <a href="/wiki/List_of_solid_waste_treatment_technologies" title="List of solid waste treatment technologies">
       waste treatment process
      </a>
      that involves the
      <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">
       combustion
      </a>
      of
      <a href="/wiki/Organic_matter" title="Organic matter">
       organic
      </a>
      substances contained in waste materials.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-1">
        [1]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Incineration and other high-temperature waste treatment systems are described as "
      <a href="/wiki/Thermal_treatment" title="Thermal treatment">
       thermal treatment
      </a>
      ". Incineration of waste materials converts the waste into
      <a href="/wiki/Incinerator_bottom_ash" title="Incinerator bottom ash">
       ash
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Flue_gas" title="Flue gas">
       flue gas
      </a>
      , and heat. The ash is mostly formed by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inorganic" title="Inorganic">
       inorganic
      </a>
      constituents of the waste, and may take the form of solid lumps or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_particulate_matter" title="Atmospheric particulate matter">
       particulates
      </a>
      carried by the flue gas. The flue gases must be cleaned of gaseous and particulate pollutants before they are dispersed into the
      <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">
       atmosphere
      </a>
      . In some cases, the heat generated by incineration can be used to generate
      <a href="/wiki/Electric_power" title="Electric power">
       electric power
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Incineration with energy recovery is one of several
      <a href="/wiki/Waste-to-energy" title="Waste-to-energy">
       waste-to-energy
      </a>
      (WtE) technologies such as
      <a href="/wiki/Gasification" title="Gasification">
       gasification
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Pyrolysis" title="Pyrolysis">
       pyrolysis
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_digestion" title="Anaerobic digestion">
       anaerobic digestion
      </a>
      . While incineration and gasification technologies are similar in principle, the energy product from incineration is high-temperature heat whereas combustible gas is often the main energy product from gasification. Incineration and gasification may also be implemented without energy and materials recovery.
     </p>
     <p>
      In several countries, there are still concerns from experts and local communities about the environmental effect of incinerators (see
      <a href="#Arguments_against_incineration">
       arguments against incineration
      </a>
      ).
     </p>
     <p>
      In some countries, incinerators built just a few decades ago often did not include a
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Material_recovery_facility" title="Material recovery facility">
       materials separation
      </a>
      to remove hazardous,
      <a href="/wiki/Bulky_waste" title="Bulky waste">
       bulky
      </a>
      or
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recyclable_waste" title="Recyclable waste">
       recyclable
      </a>
      materials before combustion. These facilities tended to risk the health of the plant workers and the local environment due to inadequate levels of gas cleaning and combustion process control. Most of these facilities did not generate electricity.
     </p>
     <p>
      Incinerators reduce the solid mass of the original waste by 80–85% and the volume (already compressed somewhat in
      <a href="/wiki/Garbage_truck" title="Garbage truck">
       garbage trucks
      </a>
      ) by 95–96%, depending on composition and degree of recovery of materials such as metals from the ash for recycling.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated7_2-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated7-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      This means that while incineration does not completely replace
      <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">
       landfilling
      </a>
      , it significantly reduces the necessary volume for disposal.
      <a href="/wiki/Garbage_truck" title="Garbage truck">
       Garbage trucks
      </a>
      often reduce the volume of waste in a built-in compressor before delivery to the incinerator. Alternatively, at landfills, the volume of the uncompressed garbage can be reduced by approximately 70%
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2009)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      by using a stationary steel compressor, albeit with a significant energy cost. In many countries, simpler
      <a href="/wiki/Waste_compaction" title="Waste compaction">
       waste compaction
      </a>
      is a common practice for compaction at landfills.
     </p>
     <p>
      Incineration has particularly strong benefits for the treatment of certain
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waste_types" title="Waste types">
       waste types
      </a>
      in
      <a href="/wiki/Niche_market" title="Niche market">
       niche
      </a>
      areas such as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clinical_waste" title="Clinical waste">
       clinical wastes
      </a>
      and certain
      <a href="/wiki/Hazardous_waste" title="Hazardous waste">
       hazardous wastes
      </a>
      where
      <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">
       pathogens
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Toxin" title="Toxin">
       toxins
      </a>
      can be destroyed by high temperatures. Examples include chemical multi-product plants with diverse toxic or very toxic wastewater streams, which cannot be routed to a conventional wastewater treatment plant.
     </p>
     <p>
      Waste combustion is particularly popular in countries such as Japan where land is a scarce resource. Denmark and Sweden have been leaders in using the energy generated from incineration for more than a century, in localised
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Combined_heat_and_power" title="Combined heat and power">
       combined heat and power
      </a>
      facilities supporting
      <a href="/wiki/District_heating" title="District heating">
       district heating
      </a>
      schemes.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-3">
        [3]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 2005, waste incineration produced 4.8% of the electricity consumption and 13.7% of the total domestic heat consumption in Denmark.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-4">
        [4]
       </a>
      </sup>
      A number of other European countries rely heavily on incineration for handling municipal waste, in particular
      <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">
       Luxembourg
      </a>
      , the Netherlands, Germany, and France.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated7_2-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated7-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Technology">
       Technology
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Incineration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Technology">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      An incinerator is a
      <a href="/wiki/Furnace" title="Furnace">
       furnace
      </a>
      for burning
      <a href="/wiki/Waste" title="Waste">
       waste
      </a>
      . Modern incinerators include pollution mitigation equipment such as
      <a href="/wiki/Flue" title="Flue">
       flue
      </a>
      gas cleaning. There are various types of incinerator plant design: moving grate, fixed grate, rotary-kiln, and fluidised bed.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Burn_pile">
       Burn pile
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Incineration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Burn pile">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Burn_pit" title="Burn pit">
       burn pile
      </a>
      is one of the simplest and earliest forms of waste disposal, essentially consisting of a mound of combustible materials piled on open ground and set on fire.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Burn_pile.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1080" data-file-width="1920" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Burn_pile.jpg/220px-Burn_pile.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Burn_pile.jpg/330px-Burn_pile.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Burn_pile.jpg/440px-Burn_pile.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Burn_pile.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        A typical small burn pile in a garden.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Burn piles can and have spread uncontrolled fires, for example if wind blows burning material off the pile into surrounding combustible grasses or onto buildings. As interior structures of the pile are consumed, the pile can shift and collapse, spreading the burn area. Even in a situation of no wind, small lightweight ignited embers can lift off the pile via
      <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">
       convection
      </a>
      , and waft through the air into grasses or onto buildings, igniting them.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Burn_barrel">
       Burn barrel
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Incineration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Burn barrel">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The burn barrel is a somewhat more controlled form of private waste incineration, containing the burning material inside a metal barrel, with a metal grating over the exhaust. The barrel prevents the spread of burning material in windy conditions, and as the combustibles are reduced they can only settle down into the barrel. The exhaust grating helps to prevent the spread of burning embers. Typically steel 55-US-gallon (210 L) drums are used as burn barrels, with air vent holes cut or drilled around the base for air intake.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
       <a href="#cite_note-8">
        [8]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Over time, the very high heat of incineration causes the metal to oxidize and rust, and eventually the barrel itself is consumed by the heat and must be replaced.
     </p>
     <p>
      Private burning of dry cellulosic/paper products is generally clean-burning, producing no visible smoke, but plastics in household waste can cause private burning to create a public nuisance, generating acrid odors and fumes that make eyes burn and water. Most urban communities ban burn barrels, and certain rural communities may have prohibitions on open burning, especially those home to many residents not familiar with this common rural practice.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      As of 2006
      <sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;">
       <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Incineration&amp;action=edit">
        [update]
       </a>
      </sup>
      in the United States, private rural household or farm waste incineration of small quantities was typically permitted so long as it is not a nuisance to others, does not pose a risk of fire such as in dry conditions, and the fire does not produce dense, noxious smoke. A handful of states, such as New York, Minnesota, and Wisconsin, have laws or regulations either banning or strictly regulating open burning due to health and nuisance effects.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA_Inventory_9-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-EPA_Inventory-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      People intending to burn waste may be required to contact a state agency in advance to check current fire risk and conditions, and to alert officials of the controlled fire that will occur.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
       <a href="#cite_note-10">
        [10]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Moving_grate">
       Moving grate
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Incineration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Moving grate">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Leitstand_2.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1387" data-file-width="2515" height="221" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Leitstand_2.jpg/400px-Leitstand_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Leitstand_2.jpg/600px-Leitstand_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Leitstand_2.jpg/800px-Leitstand_2.jpg 2x" width="400"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Leitstand_2.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Control room of a typical moving grate incinerator overseeing two boiler lines
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation">
      <tr>
       <td class="mbox-image">
        <div style="width:52px">

     <p>
      The typical incineration plant for
      <a href="/wiki/Municipal_solid_waste" title="Municipal solid waste">
       municipal solid waste
      </a>
      is a moving grate incinerator. The moving grate enables the movement of waste through the combustion chamber to be optimised to allow a more efficient and complete combustion. A single moving grate boiler can handle up to 35 metric tons (39 short tons) of waste per hour, and can operate 8,000 hours per year with only one scheduled stop for inspection and maintenance of about one month's duration. Moving grate incinerators are sometimes referred to as Municipal Solid Waste Incinerators (MSWIs).
     </p>
     <p>
      The waste is introduced by a
      <a href="/wiki/Grab_(tool)" title="Grab (tool)">
       waste crane
      </a>
      through the "throat" at one end of the grate, from where it moves down over the descending grate to the ash pit in the other end. Here the ash is removed through a water lock.
     </p>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Movinggrate.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="381" data-file-width="701" height="217" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Movinggrate.jpg/400px-Movinggrate.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Movinggrate.jpg/600px-Movinggrate.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5d/Movinggrate.jpg 2x" width="400"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Movinggrate.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Municipal solid waste in the furnace of a moving grate incinerator capable of handling 15 metric tons (17 short tons) of waste per hour. The holes in the grate elements supplying the primary combustion air are visible.
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Part of the combustion air (primary combustion air) is supplied through the grate from below. This air flow also has the purpose of cooling the grate itself. Cooling is important for the mechanical strength of the grate, and many moving grates are also water-cooled internally.
     </p>
     <p>
      Secondary combustion air is supplied into the boiler at high speed through nozzles over the grate. It facilitates complete combustion of the flue gases by introducing
      <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">
       turbulence
      </a>
      for better mixing and by ensuring a surplus of oxygen. In multiple/stepped hearth incinerators, the secondary combustion air is introduced in a separate chamber downstream the primary combustion chamber.
     </p>
     <p>
      According to the European
      <a href="/wiki/Waste_Incineration_Directive" title="Waste Incineration Directive">
       Waste Incineration Directive
      </a>
      , incineration plants must be designed to ensure that the
      <a href="/wiki/Flue_gas" title="Flue gas">
       flue gases
      </a>
      reach a temperature of at least 850 °C (1,560 °F) for 2 seconds in order to ensure proper breakdown of toxic organic substances. In order to comply with this at all times, it is required to install backup auxiliary burners (often fueled by oil), which are fired into the boiler in case the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heating_value" title="Heating value">
       heating value
      </a>
      of the waste becomes too low to reach this temperature alone.
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Flue_gas" title="Flue gas">
       flue gases
      </a>
      are then cooled in the
      <a href="/wiki/Superheater" title="Superheater">
       superheaters
      </a>
      , where the heat is transferred to steam, heating the steam to typically 400 °C (752 °F) at a pressure of 40
      <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">
       bars
      </a>
      (580
      <a href="/wiki/Pounds_per_square_inch" title="Pounds per square inch">
       psi
      </a>
      ) for the electricity generation in the
      <a href="/wiki/Turbine" title="Turbine">
       turbine
      </a>
      . At this point, the flue gas has a temperature of around 200 °C (392 °F), and is passed to the
      <a href="#Flue-gas_cleaning">
       flue gas cleaning system
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      In
      <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">
       Scandinavia
      </a>
      , scheduled maintenance is always performed during summer, where the demand for
      <a href="/wiki/District_heating" title="District heating">
       district heating
      </a>
      is low. Often, incineration plants consist of several separate 'boiler lines' (boilers and flue gas treatment plants), so that waste can continue to be received at one boiler line while the others are undergoing maintenance, repair, or upgrading.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Fixed_grate">
       Fixed grate
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Incineration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Fixed grate">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The older and simpler kind of incinerator was a brick-lined cell with a fixed metal grate over a lower ash pit, with one opening in the top or side for loading and another opening in the side for removing incombustible solids called
      <a href="/wiki/Clinker_(waste)" title="Clinker (waste)">
       clinkers
      </a>
      . Many small incinerators formerly found in apartment houses have now been replaced by
      <a href="/wiki/Compactor" title="Compactor">
       waste compactors
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
       <a href="#cite_note-11">
        [11]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">
         <span title="A more detailed citation is required.">
          full citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Rotary-kiln">
       Rotary-kiln
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Incineration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Rotary-kiln">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Rotary_dryer" title="Rotary dryer">
       rotary-kiln
      </a>
      incinerator
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
       <a href="#cite_note-12">
        [12]
       </a>
      </sup>
      is used by municipalities and by large industrial plants. This design of incinerator has 2 chambers: a primary chamber and secondary chamber. The primary chamber in a rotary kiln incinerator consist of an inclined refractory lined cylindrical tube. The inner refractory lining serves as sacrificial layer to protect the kiln structure. This refractory layer needs to be replaced from time to time.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
       <a href="#cite_note-13">
        [13]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Movement of the cylinder on its axis facilitates movement of waste. In the primary chamber, there is conversion of solid fraction to gases, through volatilization, destructive distillation and partial combustion reactions. The secondary chamber is necessary to complete gas phase combustion reactions.
     </p>
     <p>
      The clinkers spill out at the end of the cylinder. A tall flue-gas stack, fan, or steam jet supplies the needed
      <a href="/wiki/Flue-gas_stack#Flue-gas_stack_draft" title="Flue-gas stack">
       draft
      </a>
      . Ash drops through the grate, but many particles are carried along with the hot gases. The particles and any combustible gases may be combusted in an "afterburner".
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
       <a href="#cite_note-14">
        [14]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Fluidized_bed">
       Fluidized bed
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Incineration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Fluidized bed">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      A strong airflow is forced through a sandbed. The air seeps through the sand until a point is reached where the sand particles separate to let the air through and mixing and churning occurs, thus a
      <a href="/wiki/Fluidized_bed" title="Fluidized bed">
       fluidized bed
      </a>
      is created and fuel and waste can now be introduced. The sand with the pre-treated waste and/or fuel is kept suspended on pumped air currents and takes on a fluid-like character. The bed is thereby violently mixed and agitated keeping small inert particles and air in a fluid-like state. This allows all of the mass of waste, fuel and sand to be fully circulated through the furnace.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Specialized_incineration">
       Specialized incineration
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Incineration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Specialized incineration">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Furniture factory sawdust incinerators need much attention as these have to handle resin powder and many flammable substances. Controlled combustion, burn back prevention systems are essential as dust when suspended resembles the fire catch phenomenon of any liquid petroleum gas.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Use_of_heat">
       Use of heat
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Incineration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Use of heat">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The heat produced by an incinerator can be used to generate steam which may then be used to drive a
      <a href="/wiki/Turbine" title="Turbine">
       turbine
      </a>
      in order to produce electricity. The typical amount of net energy that can be produced per tonne municipal waste is about 2/3 MWh of electricity and 2 MWh of district heating.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated7_2-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated7-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Thus, incinerating about 600 metric tons (660 short tons) per day of waste will produce about 400 MWh of electrical energy per day (17
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megawatt" title="Megawatt">
       MW
      </a>
      of electrical power continuously for 24 hours) and 1200 MWh of district heating energy each day.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Pollution">
       Pollution
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Incineration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Pollution">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      Incineration has a number of outputs such as the ash and the emission to the atmosphere of
      <a href="/wiki/Flue_gas" title="Flue gas">
       flue gas
      </a>
      . Before the
      <a href="#Flue_gas_cleaning">
       flue gas cleaning system
      </a>
      , if installed, the flue gases may contain
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_particulate_matter" title="Atmospheric particulate matter">
       particulate matter
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metals" title="Heavy metals">
       heavy metals
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Polychlorinated_dibenzodioxins" title="Polychlorinated dibenzodioxins">
       dioxins
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Furans" title="Furans">
       furans
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">
       sulfur dioxide
      </a>
      , and
      <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">
       hydrochloric acid
      </a>
      . If plants have inadequate flue gas cleaning, these outputs may add a significant pollution component to stack emissions.
     </p>
     <p>
      In a study from 1997, Delaware Solid Waste Authority found that, for same amount of produced energy, incineration plants emitted fewer particles, hydrocarbons and less SO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      , HCl, CO and NO
      <sub>
       x
      </sub>
      than coal-fired power plants, but more than natural gas–fired power plants.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
       <a href="#cite_note-15">
        [15]
       </a>
      </sup>
      According to
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_Ministry_for_Environment,_Nature_Conservation_and_Nuclear_Safety" title="Federal Ministry for Environment, Nature Conservation and Nuclear Safety">
       Germany's Ministry of the Environment
      </a>
      , waste incinerators reduce the amount of some atmospheric pollutants by substituting power produced by coal-fired plants with power from waste-fired plants.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_16-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Gaseous_emissions">
       Gaseous emissions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Incineration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Gaseous emissions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Dioxin_and_furans">
       Dioxin and furans
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Incineration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Dioxin and furans">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      The most publicized concerns from environmentalists about the incineration of municipal solid wastes (MSW) involve the fear that it produces significant amounts of
      <a href="/wiki/Polychlorinated_dibenzodioxins" title="Polychlorinated dibenzodioxins">
       dioxin
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Furan" title="Furan">
       furan
      </a>
      emissions.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
       <a href="#cite_note-17">
        [17]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Dioxins and furans are considered by many to be serious health hazards. The EPA announced in 2012 that the safe limit for human oral consumption is 0.7 picograms Toxic Equivalence (TEQ) per kilogram bodyweight per day,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
       <a href="#cite_note-18">
        [18]
       </a>
      </sup>
      which works out to 17 billionths of a gram for a 150 lb person per year.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2005, The Ministry of the Environment of Germany, where there were 66 incinerators at that time, estimated that "...whereas in 1990 one third of all dioxin emissions in Germany came from incineration plants, for the year 2000 the figure was less than 1%.
      <a href="/wiki/Chimney" title="Chimney">
       Chimneys
      </a>
      and tiled stoves in private households alone discharge approximately 20 times more dioxin into the environment than incineration plants."
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_16-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-16">
        [16]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      According to the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">
       United States Environmental Protection Agency
      </a>
      ,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA_Inventory_9-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-EPA_Inventory-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      the combustion percentages of the total dioxin and furan inventory from all known and estimated sources in the U.S. (not only incineration) for each type of incineration are as follows: 35.1% backyard barrels; 26.6% medical waste; 6.3% municipal
      <a href="/wiki/Sewage_sludge" title="Sewage sludge">
       wastewater treatment sludge
      </a>
      ; 5.9% municipal waste combustion; 2.9% industrial wood combustion. Thus, the controlled combustion of waste accounted for 41.7% of the total dioxin inventory.
     </p>
     <p>
      In 1987, before the governmental regulations required the use of emission controls, there was a total of 8,905.1 grams (314.12 oz) Toxic Equivalence (TEQ) of dioxin emissions from US municipal waste combustors. Today, the total emissions from the plants are 83.8 grams (2.96 oz) TEQ annually, a reduction of 99%.
     </p>
     <p>
      Backyard barrel burning of household and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Garden_waste" title="Garden waste">
       garden wastes
      </a>
      , still allowed in some rural areas, generates 580 grams (20 oz) of dioxins annually. Studies conducted by the US-EPA
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
       <a href="#cite_note-19">
        [19]
       </a>
      </sup>
      demonstrated that the emissions from just one family using a burn barrel produced more emissions than an incineration plant disposing of 200 metric tons (220 short tons) of waste per day by 1997 and five times that by 2007 due to increased chemicals in household trash and decreased emissions by municipal incinerators using better technology.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
       <a href="#cite_note-20">
        [20]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      However, the same researchers found that their original estimates for the burn barrel were high, and that the incineration plant used for comparison represented a theoretical 'clean' plant rather than any existing facility. Their later studies
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
       <a href="#cite_note-21">
        [21]
       </a>
      </sup>
      found that burn barrels produced a median of 24.95 nanograms TEQ per lb garbage burned, so that a family burning 5 lbs of trash per day, or 1825 lbs per year, produces a total of 0.0455 mg TEQ per year, and that the equivalent number of burn barrels for the 83.8 grams (2.96 oz) of the 251 municipal waste combustors inventoried by the EPA in the U.S. in 2000,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA_Inventory_9-2">
       <a href="#cite_note-EPA_Inventory-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
      is 1,841,700, or on average, 7337 family burn barrels per municipal waste incinerator.
     </p>
     <p>
      Most of the improvement in U.S. dioxin emissions has been for large-scale municipal waste incinerators. As of the year 2000, although small-scale incinerators (those with a daily capacity of less than 250 tons) processed only 9% of the total waste combusted, these produced 83% of the dioxins and furans emitted by municipal waste combustion.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA_Inventory_9-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-EPA_Inventory-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>

     <p>
      The breakdown of dioxin requires exposure of the molecular ring to a sufficiently high temperature so as to trigger thermal breakdown of the strong molecular bonds holding it together. Small pieces of fly ash may be somewhat thick, and too brief an exposure to high temperature may only degrade dioxin on the surface of the ash. For a large volume air chamber, too brief an exposure may also result in only some of the exhaust gases reaching the full breakdown temperature. For this reason there is also a time element to the temperature exposure to ensure heating completely through the thickness of the fly ash and the volume of waste gases.
     </p>
     <p>
      There are trade-offs between increasing either the temperature or exposure time. Generally where the molecular breakdown temperature is higher, the exposure time for heating can be shorter, but excessively high temperatures can also cause wear and damage to other parts of the incineration equipment. Likewise the breakdown temperature can be lowered to some degree but then the exhaust gases would require a greater lingering period of perhaps several minutes, which would require large/long treatment chambers that take up a great deal of treatment plant space.
     </p>
     <p>
      A side effect of breaking the strong molecular bonds of dioxin is the potential for breaking the bonds of nitrogen gas (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrogen_gas" title="Nitrogen gas">
       N
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
      </a>
      ) and oxygen gas (
      <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">
       O
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
      </a>
      ) in the supply air. As the exhaust flow cools, these highly reactive detached atoms spontaneously reform bonds into reactive oxides such as
      <a href="/wiki/NOx" title="NOx">
       NO
       <sub>
        x
       </sub>
      </a>
      in the flue gas, which can result in smog formation and
      <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">
       acid rain
      </a>
      if they were released directly into the local environment. These reactive oxides must be further neutralized with
      <a href="/wiki/Selective_catalytic_reduction" title="Selective catalytic reduction">
       selective catalytic reduction
      </a>
      (SCR) or
      <a href="/wiki/Selective_non-catalytic_reduction" title="Selective non-catalytic reduction">
       selective non-catalytic reduction
      </a>
      (see below).
     </p>

     <p>
      The temperatures needed to break down dioxin are typically not reached when burning plastics outdoors in a burn barrel or garbage pit, causing high dioxin emissions as mentioned above. While plastic does usually burn in an open-air fire, the dioxins remain after combustion and either float off into the atmosphere, or may remain in the ash where it can be leached down into groundwater when rain falls on the ash pile. Fortunately, dioxin and furan compounds bond very strongly to solid surfaces and are not dissolved by water, so leaching processes are limited to the first few milimeters below the ash pile. The gas-phase dioxins can be substantially destroyed using catalysts, some of which can be present as part of the fabric filter bag structure.
     </p>
     <p>
      Modern municipal incinerator designs include a high-temperature zone, where the flue gas is sustained at a temperature above 850 °C (1,560 °F) for at least 2 seconds before it is cooled down. They are equipped with auxiliary heaters to ensure this at all times. These are often fueled by oil or natural gas, and are normally only active for a very small fraction of the time. Further, most modern incinerators utilize fabric filters (often with Teflon membranes to enhance collection of sub-micron particles) which can capture dioxins present in or on solid particles.
     </p>
     <p>
      For very small municipal incinerators, the required temperature for thermal breakdown of dioxin may be reached using a high-temperature electrical heating element, plus a
      <a href="/wiki/Selective_catalytic_reduction" title="Selective catalytic reduction">
       selective catalytic reduction
      </a>
      stage.
     </p>
     <p>
      Although dioxins and furans may be destroyed by combustion, their reformation by a process known as 'de novo synthesis' as the emission gases cool is a probable source of the dioxins measured in emission stack tests from plants that have high combustion temperatures held at long residence times.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA_Inventory_9-4">
       <a href="#cite_note-EPA_Inventory-9">
        [9]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="CO2">
       CO
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Incineration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: CO2">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      As for other complete combustion processes, nearly all of the carbon content in the waste is emitted as CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      to the atmosphere.
      <a href="/wiki/Municipal_solid_waste" title="Municipal solid waste">
       MSW
      </a>
      contains approximately the same mass fraction of carbon as CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      itself (27%), so incineration of 1 ton of MSW produces approximately 1 ton of CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      If the waste was
      <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">
       landfilled
      </a>
      , 1 ton of MSW would produce approximately 62 cubic metres (2,200 cu ft)
      <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">
       methane
      </a>
      via the
      <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_digestion" title="Anaerobic digestion">
       anaerobic
      </a>
      decomposition of the
      <a href="/wiki/Biodegradable_waste" title="Biodegradable waste">
       biodegradable
      </a>
      part of the waste. Since the
      <a href="/wiki/Global_warming_potential" title="Global warming potential">
       global warming potential
      </a>
      of methane is 34 and the weight of 62 cubic meters of methane at 25 degrees Celsius is 40.7 kg, this is equivalent to 1.38 ton of CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      , which is more than the 1 ton of CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      which would have been produced by incineration. In some countries, large amounts of
      <a href="/wiki/Landfill_gas" title="Landfill gas">
       landfill gas
      </a>
      are collected. Still the global warming potential of the landfill gas emitted to atmosphere is significant. In the US it was estimated that the global warming potential of the emitted landfill gas in 1999 was approximately 32% higher than the amount of CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      that would have been emitted by incineration.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_22-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-22">
        [22]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Since this study, the global warming potential estimate for methane has been increased from 21 to 35, which alone would increase this estimate to almost the triple GWP effect compared to incineration of the same waste.
     </p>
     <p>
      In addition, nearly all biodegradable waste has biological origin. This material has been formed by plants using atmospheric CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      typically within the last growing season. If these plants are regrown the CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      emitted from their combustion will be taken out from the atmosphere once more.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Such considerations are the main reason why several countries administrate incineration of biodegradable waste as
      <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">
       renewable energy
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
       <a href="#cite_note-23">
        [23]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The rest – mainly plastics and other oil and gas derived products – is generally treated as
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-renewable_resources" title="Non-renewable resources">
       non-renewables
      </a>
      .
     </p>
     <p>
      Different results for the CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      footprint of incineration can be reached with different assumptions. Local conditions (such as limited local district heating demand, no fossil fuel generated electricity to replace or high levels of aluminium in the waste stream) can decrease the CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      benefits of incineration. The methodology and other assumptions may also influence the results significantly. For example, the methane emissions from landfills occurring at a later date may be neglected or given less weight, or biodegradable waste may not be considered CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      neutral. A study by Eunomia Research and Consulting in 2008 on potential waste treatment technologies in London demonstrated that by applying several of these (according to the authors) unusual assumptions the average existing incineration plants performed poorly for CO
      <sub>
       2
      </sub>
      balance compared to the theoretical potential of other emerging waste treatment technologies.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_24-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-24">
        [24]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_emissions">
       Other emissions
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Incineration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Other emissions">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <p>
      Other gaseous emissions in the flue gas from incinerator furnaces include
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxides" title="Nitrogen oxides">
       nitrogen oxides
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">
       sulfur dioxide
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">
       hydrochloric acid
      </a>
      ,
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_(chemistry)" title="Heavy metal (chemistry)">
       heavy metals
      </a>
      , and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fine_particles" title="Fine particles">
       fine particles
      </a>
      . Of the heavy metals,
      <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">
       mercury
      </a>
      is a major concern due to its toxicity and high volatility, as essentially all mercury in the municipal waste stream may exit in emissions if not removed by emission controls.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chang_2003_25-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-Chang_2003-25">
        [25]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The
      <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">
       steam
      </a>
      content in the flue may produce visible fume from the stack, which can be perceived as a
      <a href="/wiki/Visual_pollution" title="Visual pollution">
       visual pollution
      </a>
      . It may be avoided by decreasing the steam content by
      <a href="/wiki/Flue-gas_condensation" title="Flue-gas condensation">
       flue-gas condensation
      </a>
      and reheating, or by increasing the flue gas exit temperature well above its dew point. Flue-gas condensation allows the latent heat of vaporization of the water to be recovered, subsequently increasing the thermal efficiency of the plant.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h4>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Flue-gas_cleaning">
       Flue-gas cleaning
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Incineration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Flue-gas cleaning">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h4>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Inside_of_the_electrostatic_precipitator.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2736" data-file-width="3648" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Inside_of_the_electrostatic_precipitator.jpg/220px-Inside_of_the_electrostatic_precipitator.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Inside_of_the_electrostatic_precipitator.jpg/330px-Inside_of_the_electrostatic_precipitator.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Inside_of_the_electrostatic_precipitator.jpg/440px-Inside_of_the_electrostatic_precipitator.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Inside_of_the_electrostatic_precipitator.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Electrodes inside
        <a href="/wiki/Electrostatic_precipitator" title="Electrostatic precipitator">
         electrostatic precipitator
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      The quantity of pollutants in the flue gas from incineration plants may or may not be reduced by several processes, depending on the plant.
     </p>
     <p>
      Particulate is collected by
      <a href="/wiki/Dust_collector" title="Dust collector">
       particle filtration
      </a>
      , most often
      <a href="/wiki/Electrostatic_precipitator" title="Electrostatic precipitator">
       electrostatic precipitators
      </a>
      (ESP) and/or
      <a href="/wiki/Dust_collector#Fabric_Collectors" title="Dust collector">
       baghouse filters
      </a>
      . The latter are generally very efficient for collecting
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fine_particles" title="Fine particles">
       fine particles
      </a>
      . In an investigation by the
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ministry_of_the_Environment_of_Denmark" title="Ministry of the Environment of Denmark">
       Ministry of the Environment of Denmark
      </a>
      in 2006, the average particulate emissions per energy content of incinerated waste from 16 Danish incinerators were below 2.02 g/GJ (grams per energy content of the incinerated waste). Detailed measurements of fine particles with sizes below 2.5
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Micrometres" title="Micrometres">
       micrometres
      </a>
      (
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PM2.5" title="PM2.5">
       PM
       <sub>
        2.5
       </sub>
      </a>
      ) were performed on three of the incinerators: One incinerator equipped with an ESP for particle filtration emitted 5.3 g/GJ fine particles, while two incinerators equipped with baghouse filters emitted 0.002 and 0.013 g/GJ PM
      <sub>
       2.5
      </sub>
      . For ultra fine particles (PM
      <sub>
       1.0
      </sub>
      ), the numbers were 4.889 g/GJ PM
      <sub>
       1.0
      </sub>
      from the ESP plant, while emissions of 0.000 and 0.008 g/GJ PM
      <sub>
       1.0
      </sub>
      were measured from the plants equipped with baghouse filters.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PMemissions25_26-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-PMemissions25-26">
        [26]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_27-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-27">
        [27]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Acid gas
      <a href="/wiki/Wet_scrubber" title="Wet scrubber">
       scrubbers
      </a>
      are used to remove
      <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">
       hydrochloric acid
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">
       nitric acid
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid" title="Hydrofluoric acid">
       hydrofluoric acid
      </a>
      ,
      <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">
       mercury
      </a>
      , lead and other
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_(chemistry)" title="Heavy metal (chemistry)">
       heavy metals
      </a>
      . The efficiency of removal will depend on the specific equipment, the chemical composition of the waste, the design of the plant, the chemistry of reagents, and the ability of engineers to optimize these conditions, which may conflict for different pollutants. For example, mercury removal by wet scrubbers is considered coincidental and may be less than 50%.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chang_2003_25-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-Chang_2003-25">
        [25]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Basic scrubbers remove
      <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">
       sulfur dioxide
      </a>
      , forming
      <a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">
       gypsum
      </a>
      by reaction with
      <a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">
       lime
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
       <a href="#cite_note-28">
        [28]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Waste water from scrubbers must subsequently pass through a waste water treatment plant.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Sulfur dioxide may also be removed by dry
      <a href="/wiki/Flue-gas_desulfurization" title="Flue-gas desulfurization">
       desulfurisation
      </a>
      by injection
      <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">
       limestone
      </a>
      <a href="/wiki/Slurry" title="Slurry">
       slurry
      </a>
      into the flue gas before the particle filtration.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/NOx" title="NOx">
       NO
       <sub>
        x
       </sub>
      </a>
      is either reduced by catalytic reduction with ammonia in a
      <a href="/wiki/Catalytic_converter" title="Catalytic converter">
       catalytic converter
      </a>
      (
      <a href="/wiki/Selective_catalytic_reduction" title="Selective catalytic reduction">
       selective catalytic reduction
      </a>
      , SCR) or by a high-temperature reaction with ammonia in the furnace (
      <a href="/wiki/Selective_non-catalytic_reduction" title="Selective non-catalytic reduction">
       selective non-catalytic reduction
      </a>
      , SNCR). Urea may be substituted for ammonia as the reducing reagent but must be supplied earlier in the process so that it can hydrolyze into ammonia. Substitution of urea can reduce costs and potential hazards associated with storage of anhydrous ammonia.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Heavy metals are often
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adsorb" title="Adsorb">
       adsorbed
      </a>
      on injected
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Active_carbon" title="Active carbon">
       active carbon
      </a>
      powder, which is collected by particle filtration.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Solid_outputs">

      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Incineration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Solid outputs">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:US_Navy_081003-N-2456S-020_Aviation_Electronics_Technician_Airman_Eric_Syck_burns_trash_in_the_incinerator_aboard_the_aircraft_carrier_USS_Theodore_Roosevelt_(CVN_71).jpg">

     <p>
      Incineration produces
      <a href="/wiki/Fly_ash" title="Fly ash">
       fly ash
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Bottom_ash" title="Bottom ash">
       bottom ash
      </a>
      just as is the case when coal is combusted. The total amount of ash produced by municipal solid waste incineration ranges from 4 to 10% by volume and 15–20% by weight of the original quantity of waste,
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated7_2-3">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated7-2">
        [2]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated5_29-0">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated5-29">
        [29]
       </a>
      </sup>
      and the fly ash amounts to about 10–20% of the total ash.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2007)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      The fly ash, by far, constitutes more of a potential health hazard than does the bottom ash because the fly ash often contain high concentrations of heavy metals such as lead,
      <a href="/wiki/Cadmium" title="Cadmium">
       cadmium
      </a>
      , copper and
      <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">
       zinc
      </a>
      as well as small amounts of dioxins and furans.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
       <a href="#cite_note-30">
        [30]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The bottom ash seldom contain significant levels of heavy metals. In testing over the past decade, no ash from an incineration plant in the USA has ever been determined to be a
      <a href="/wiki/Hazardous_waste" title="Hazardous waste">
       hazardous waste
      </a>
      .
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2007)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      At present although some historic samples tested by the incinerator operators' group would meet the being ecotoxic criteria at present the EA say "we have agreed" to regard incinerator bottom ash as "non-hazardous" until the testing programme is complete.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2007)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_pollution_issues">
       Other pollution issues
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Incineration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Other pollution issues">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      <a href="/wiki/Odor" title="Odor">
       Odor
      </a>
      pollution can be a problem with old-style incinerators, but odors and dust are extremely well controlled in newer incineration plants. They receive and store the waste in an enclosed area with a negative pressure with the airflow being routed through the boiler which prevents
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unpleasant_odor" title="Unpleasant odor">
       unpleasant odors
      </a>
      from escaping into the atmosphere. However, not all plants are implemented this way, resulting in inconveniences in the locality.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      An issue that affects community relationships is the increased road traffic of
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waste_collection_vehicle" title="Waste collection vehicle">
       waste collection vehicles
      </a>
      to transport municipal waste to the incinerator. Due to this reason, most incinerators are located in industrial areas. This problem can be avoided to an extent through the transport of waste by rail from transfer stations.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Debate">
       Debate
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Incineration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Debate">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      Use of incinerators for
      <a href="/wiki/Waste_management" title="Waste management">
       waste management
      </a>
      is controversial. The debate over incinerators typically involves business interests (representing both waste generators and incinerator firms), government regulators, environmental activists and local citizens who must weigh the economic appeal of local industrial activity with their concerns over health and environmental risk.
     </p>
     <p>
      People and organizations professionally involved in this issue include the
      <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">
       U.S. Environmental Protection Agency
      </a>
      and a great many local and national air quality regulatory agencies worldwide.
     </p>

        </i>
        in Hinwil, Switzerland
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <ul>
      <li>
       The concerns over the health effects of
       <a href="/wiki/Polychlorinated_dibenzodioxins" title="Polychlorinated dibenzodioxins">
        dioxin
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Furan" title="Furan">
        furan
       </a>
       emissions have been significantly lessened by advances in emission control designs and very stringent new governmental regulations that have resulted in large reductions in the amount of dioxins and furans emissions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_16-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       The U.K.
       <a href="/wiki/Health_Protection_Agency" title="Health Protection Agency">
        Health Protection Agency
       </a>
       concluded in 2009 that "Modern, well managed incinerators make only a small contribution to local concentrations of air pollutants. It is possible that such small additions could have an impact on health but such effects, if they exist, are likely to be very small and not detectable."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HPA2009_31-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-HPA2009-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       Incineration plants can generate electricity and heat that can substitute power plants powered by other fuels at the regional electric and
       <a href="/wiki/District_heating" title="District heating">
        district heating
       </a>
       grid, and steam supply for industrial customers. Incinerators and other waste-to-energy plants generate at least partially biomass-based renewable energy that offsets greenhouse gas pollution from coal-, oil- and gas-fired power plants.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
        <a href="#cite_note-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The E.U. considers energy generated from biogenic waste (waste with biological origin) by incinerators as non-fossil renewable energy under its emissions caps. These greenhouse gas reductions are in addition to those generated by the avoidance of landfill methane.
      </li>
      <li>
       The bottom ash residue remaining after combustion has been shown to be a non-hazardous solid waste that can be safely put into landfills or recycled as construction aggregate. Samples are tested for ecotoxic metals.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated6_33-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated6-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       In densely populated areas, finding space for additional landfills is becoming increasingly difficult.
      </li>
      <li>
       <div class="thumb tright">
        <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
         <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Maishima_waste_treatment_center_Osaka_JPN_002.jpg">
          <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2161" data-file-width="2171" height="219" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Maishima_waste_treatment_center_Osaka_JPN_002.jpg/220px-Maishima_waste_treatment_center_Osaka_JPN_002.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Maishima_waste_treatment_center_Osaka_JPN_002.jpg/330px-Maishima_waste_treatment_center_Osaka_JPN_002.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Maishima_waste_treatment_center_Osaka_JPN_002.jpg/440px-Maishima_waste_treatment_center_Osaka_JPN_002.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
         </a>
         <div class="thumbcaption">
          <div class="magnify">
           <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Maishima_waste_treatment_center_Osaka_JPN_002.jpg" title="Enlarge">
           </a>
          </div>
          The Maishima waste treatment center in Osaka, designed by Friedensreich Hundertwasser, uses heat for power generation.
         </div>
        </div>
       </div>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fine_particles" title="Fine particles">
        Fine particles
       </a>
       can be efficiently removed from the flue gases with
       <a href="/wiki/Baghouse" title="Baghouse">
        baghouse filters
       </a>
       . Even though approximately 40% of the incinerated waste in Denmark was incinerated at plants with no baghouse filters, estimates based on measurements by the Danish Environmental Research Institute showed that incinerators were only responsible for approximately 0.3% of the total domestic emissions of particulate smaller than 2.5
       <a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">
        micrometres
       </a>
       (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PM2.5" title="PM2.5">
        PM
        <sub>
         2.5
        </sub>
       </a>
       ) to the atmosphere in 2006.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PMemissions25_26-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-PMemissions25-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_27-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       Incineration of municipal solid waste avoids the release of
       <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">
        methane
       </a>
       . Every ton of MSW incinerated, prevents about one ton of carbon dioxide equivalents from being released to the atmosphere.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_22-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       Most municipalities that operate incineration facilities have higher recycling rates than neighboring cities and counties that do not send their waste to incinerators.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
        <a href="#cite_note-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">
          <span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (January 2015)">
           not in citation given
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       This is in part due to enhanced recovery of ceramic materials reused in construction, as well as ferrous and in some cases non-ferrous metals that can be recovered from combustion residue.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
        <a href="#cite_note-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Metals recovered from ash would typically be difficult or impossible to recycle through conventional means, as the removal of attached combustible material through incineration provides an alternative to labor- or energy-intensive mechanical separation methods.
      </li>
      <li>
       Volume of combusted waste is reduced by approximately 90%, increasing the life of landfills. Ash from modern incinerators is vitrified at temperatures of 1,000 °C (1,830 °F) to 1,100 °C (2,010 °F), reducing the leachability and toxicity of residue. As a result, special landfills are generally no longer required for incinerator ash from municipal waste streams, and existing landfills can see their life dramatically increased by combusting waste, reducing the need for municipalities to site and construct new landfills.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
        <a href="#cite_note-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
        <a href="#cite_note-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Arguments_against_incineration">
       Arguments against incineration
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Incineration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Arguments against incineration">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kwai_Chung_Incineration_Plant.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2560" data-file-width="1920" height="400" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Kwai_Chung_Incineration_Plant.jpg/300px-Kwai_Chung_Incineration_Plant.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Kwai_Chung_Incineration_Plant.jpg/450px-Kwai_Chung_Incineration_Plant.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Kwai_Chung_Incineration_Plant.jpg/600px-Kwai_Chung_Incineration_Plant.jpg 2x" width="300"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">

        <a href="/wiki/Kwai_Chung_Incineration_Plant" title="Kwai Chung Incineration Plant">

       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <ul>
      <li>
       The Scottish Protection Agency's (SEPA) comprehensive health effects research concluded "inconclusively" on health effects in October 2009. The authors stress, that even though no conclusive evidence of non-occupational health effects from incinerators were found in the existing literature, "small but important effects might be virtually impossible to detect". The report highlights epidemiological deficiencies in previous UK health studies and suggests areas for future studies.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
        <a href="#cite_note-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The U.K.
       <a href="/wiki/Health_Protection_Agency" title="Health Protection Agency">
        Health Protection Agency
       </a>
       produced a lesser summary in September 2009.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HPA2009_31-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-HPA2009-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Many toxiocologists criticise and dispute this report as not being comprehensive epidemiologically, thin on peer review and the effects of fine particle effects on health.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       The highly toxic
       <a href="/wiki/Fly_ash" title="Fly ash">
        fly ash
       </a>
       must be safely disposed of. This usually involves additional waste miles and the need for specialist toxic waste landfill elsewhere. If not done properly, it may cause concerns for local residents.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
        <a href="#cite_note-39">
         [39]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
        <a href="#cite_note-40">
         [40]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       The health effects of
       <a href="/wiki/Polychlorinated_dibenzodioxins" title="Polychlorinated dibenzodioxins">
        dioxin
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Furan" title="Furan">
        furan
       </a>
       emissions from old incinerators; especially during start up and shut down, or where filter bypass is required continue to be a problem.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       Incinerators emit varying levels of heavy metals such as
       <a href="/wiki/Vanadium" title="Vanadium">
        vanadium
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">
        manganese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">
        chromium
       </a>
       , nickel,
       <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">
        arsenic
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">
        mercury
       </a>
       , lead, and
       <a href="/wiki/Cadmium" title="Cadmium">
        cadmium
       </a>
       , which can be toxic at very minute levels.
      </li>
      <li>
       Incinerator Bottom Ash (IBA) has elevated levels of heavy metals with ecotoxicity concerns if not reused properly. Some people have the opinion that IBA reuse is still in its infancy and is still not considered to be a mature or desirable product, despite additional engineering treatments. Concerns of IBA use in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foam_Concrete" title="Foam Concrete">
        Foam Concrete
       </a>
       have been expressed by the UK Health and Safety Executive in 2010 following several construction and demolition explosions. In its guidance document, IBA is currently banned from use by the UK Highway Authority in concrete work until these incidents have been investigated.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
        <a href="#cite_note-41">
         [41]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       Alternative technologies are available or in development such as
       <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_biological_treatment" title="Mechanical biological treatment">
        mechanical biological treatment
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_digestion" title="Anaerobic digestion">
        anaerobic digestion
       </a>
       (MBT/AD),
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autoclaving" title="Autoclaving">
        autoclaving
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_heat_treatment" title="Mechanical heat treatment">
        mechanical heat treatment
       </a>
       (MHT) using steam or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plasma_arc_gasification" title="Plasma arc gasification">
        plasma arc gasification
       </a>
       (PGP), which is incineration using electrically produced extreme high temperatures, or combinations of these treatments.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       Erection of incinerators compete with the development and introduction of other emerging technologies. A UK government WRAP report, August 2008 found that in the UK median incinerator costs per ton were generally higher than those for MBT treatments by £18 per
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metric_ton" title="Metric ton">
        metric ton
       </a>
       ; and £27 per metric ton for most modern (post 2000) incinerators.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
        <a href="#cite_note-42">
         [42]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
        <a href="#cite_note-43">
         [43]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       Building and operating waste processing plants such as incinerators requires long contract periods to recover initial investment costs, causing a long term lock-in. Incinerator lifetimes normally range 25–30 years. This was highlighted by Peter Jones,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OBE" title="OBE">
        OBE
       </a>
       , the Mayor of London's waste representative in April 2009.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
        <a href="#cite_note-44">
         [44]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       Incinerators produce fine particles in the furnace. Even with modern particle filtering of the flue gases, a small part of these is emitted to the atmosphere. PM
       <sub>
        2.5
       </sub>
       is not separately regulated in the European
       <a href="/wiki/Waste_Incineration_Directive" title="Waste Incineration Directive">
        Waste Incineration Directive
       </a>
       , even though they are repeatedly correlated spatially to infant mortality in the UK (M. Ryan's ONS data based maps around the EfW/CHP waste incinerators at Edmonton, Coventry, Chineham, Kirklees and Sheffield).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
        <a href="#cite_note-45">
         [45]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
        <a href="#cite_note-46">
         [46]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
        <a href="#cite_note-47">
         [47]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Under WID there is no requirement to monitor stack top or downwind incinerator PM
       <sub>
        2.5
       </sub>
       levels.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
        <a href="#cite_note-48">
         [48]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS">
          <span title="This claim needs references to better sources. (January 2015)">
           better source needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Several European doctors associations (including cross discipline experts such as physicians, environmental chemists and toxicologists) in June 2008 representing over 33,000 doctors wrote a keynote statement directly to the European Parliament citing widespread concerns on incinerator particle emissions and the absence of specific fine and ultrafine particle size monitoring or in depth industry/government epidemiological studies of these minute and invisible incinerator particle size emissions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
        <a href="#cite_note-49">
         [49]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       Local communities are often opposed to the idea of locating waste processing plants such as incinerators in their vicinity (the
       <a href="/wiki/NIMBY" title="NIMBY">
        Not in My Back Yard
       </a>
       phenomenon). Studies in
       <a href="/wiki/Andover,_Massachusetts" title="Andover, Massachusetts">
        Andover, Massachusetts
       </a>
       correlated 10% property devaluations with close incinerator proximity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
        <a href="#cite_note-50">
         [50]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       Prevention,
       <a href="/wiki/Waste_minimisation" title="Waste minimisation">
        waste minimisation
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Reuse" title="Reuse">
        reuse
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Recycling" title="Recycling">
        recycling
       </a>
       of waste should all be preferred to incineration according to the
       <a href="/wiki/Waste_hierarchy" title="Waste hierarchy">
        waste hierarchy
       </a>
       . Supporters of
       <a href="/wiki/Zero_waste" title="Zero waste">
        zero waste
       </a>
       consider incinerators and other waste treatment technologies as barriers to
       <a href="/wiki/Recycling" title="Recycling">
        recycling
       </a>
       and separation beyond particular levels, and that waste resources are sacrificed for energy production.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
        <a href="#cite_note-51">
         [51]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
        <a href="#cite_note-52">
         [52]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
        <a href="#cite_note-53">
         [53]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       A 2008 Eunomia report found that under some circumstances and assumptions, incineration causes less CO
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       reduction than other emerging
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EfW" title="EfW">
        EfW
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">
        CHP
       </a>
       technology combinations for treating residual mixed waste.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_24-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The authors found that CHP incinerator technology without waste recycling ranked 19 out of 24 combinations (where all alternatives to incineration were combined with advanced waste recycling plants); being 228% less efficient than the ranked 1 Advanced MBT maturation technology; or 211% less efficient than plasma gasification/autoclaving combination ranked 2.
      </li>
      <li>
       Some incinerators are visually undesirable. In many countries they require a visually intrusive chimney stack.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       If reusable waste fractions are handled in waste processing plants such as incinerators in developing nations, it would cut out viable work for local economies. It is estimated that there are 1 million people making a livelihood off collecting waste.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Medina.2C_M._2000_51.E2.80.9369_54-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Medina.2C_M._2000_51.E2.80.9369-54">
         [54]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       The reduced levels of emissions from municipal waste incinerators and waste to energy plants from historical peaks are largely the product of the proficient use of emission control technology. Emission controls add to the initial and operational expenses. It should not be assumed that all new plants will employ the best available control technology if not required by law.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">

        <i>

        ]
       </sup>
      </li>

     <h2>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Trends_in_incinerator_use">
       Trends in incinerator use
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Incineration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Trends in incinerator use">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h2>
     <p>
      The history of
      <a href="/wiki/Municipal_solid_waste" title="Municipal solid waste">
       municipal solid waste
      </a>
      (MSW) incineration is linked intimately to the history of
      <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">
       landfills
      </a>
      and other
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_waste_treatment_technologies" title="List of waste treatment technologies">
       waste treatment technology
      </a>
      . The merits of incineration are inevitably judged in relation to the alternatives available. Since the 1970s, recycling and other prevention measures have changed the context for such judgements. Since the 1990s alternative waste treatment technologies have been maturing and becoming viable.
     </p>
     <p>
      Incineration is a key process in the treatment of hazardous wastes and clinical wastes. It is often imperative that medical waste be subjected to the high temperatures of incineration to destroy
      <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">
       pathogens
      </a>
      and
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toxic" title="Toxic">
       toxic
      </a>
      contamination it contains.
     </p>

      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [

        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      The first incinerator in the U.S. was built in 1885 on Governors Island in New York.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">
       <a href="#cite_note-55">
        [55]
       </a>
      </sup>
      In 1949, Robert C. Ross founded one of the first hazardous waste management companies in the U.S. He began Robert Ross Industrial Disposal because he saw an opportunity to meet the hazardous waste management needs of companies in northern Ohio. In 1958, the company built one of the first hazardous waste incinerators in the U.S.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">
       <a href="#cite_note-56">
        [56]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      The first full-scale, municipally operated incineration facility in the U.S. was the Arnold O. Chantland Resource Recovery Plant, built in 1975 and located in
      <a href="/wiki/Ames,_Iowa" title="Ames, Iowa">
       Ames, Iowa
      </a>
      . This plant is still in operation and produces
      <a href="/wiki/Refuse-derived_fuel" title="Refuse-derived fuel">
       refuse-derived fuel
      </a>
      that is sent to local power plants for fuel.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57">
       <a href="#cite_note-57">
        [57]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The first commercially successful incineration plant in the U.S. was built in
      <a href="/wiki/Saugus,_Massachusetts" title="Saugus, Massachusetts">
       Saugus, Massachusetts
      </a>
      in October 1975 by Wheelabrator Technologies, and is still in operation today.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated5_29-1">
       <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated5-29">
        [29]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      There are several environmental or waste management corporations that transport ultimately to an incinerator or cement kiln treatment center. Currently (2009), there are three main businesses that incinerate waste: Clean Harbours, WTI-Heritage, and Ross Incineration Services. Clean Harbours has acquired many of the smaller, independently run facilities, accumulating 5–7 incinerators in the process across the U.S. WTI-Heritage has one incinerator, located in the southeastern corner of
      <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">
       Ohio
      </a>
      across the Ohio River from West Virginia.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Several old generation incinerators have been closed; of the 186 MSW incinerators in 1990, only 89 remained by 2007, and of the 6200 medical waste incinerators in 1988, only 115 remained in 2003.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58">
       <a href="#cite_note-58">
        [58]
       </a>
      </sup>
      No new incinerators were built between 1996 and 2007.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
      The main reasons for lack of activity have been:
     </p>
     <ul>
      <li>
       Economics. With the increase in the number of large inexpensive regional landfills and, up until recently, the relatively low price of electricity, incinerators were not able to compete for the 'fuel', i.e., waste in the U.S.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </li>
      <li>
       Tax policies. Tax credits for plants producing electricity from waste were rescinded in the U.S. between 1990 and 2004.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </li>
     </ul>
     <p>
      There has been renewed interest in incineration and other waste-to-energy technologies in the U.S. and Canada. In the U.S., incineration was granted qualification for
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renewable_energy_development#Production_tax_credits" title="Renewable energy development">
       renewable energy production tax credits
      </a>
      in 2004.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59">
       <a href="#cite_note-59">
        [59]
       </a>
      </sup>
      Projects to add capacity to existing plants are underway, and municipalities are once again evaluating the option of building incineration plants rather than continue landfilling municipal wastes. However, many of these projects have faced continued political opposition in spite of renewed arguments for the greenhouse gas benefits of incineration and improved air pollution control and ash recycling.
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Incineration_in_Europe">
       Incineration in Europe
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Incineration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Incineration in Europe">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <p>
      In Europe, with the ban on landfilling untreated waste, scores of incinerators have been built in the last decade, with more under construction. Recently, a number of municipal governments have begun the process of contracting for the construction and operation of incinerators. In Europe, some of the electricity generated from waste is deemed to be from a 'Renewable Energy Source (RES) and is thus eligible for tax credits if privately operated. Also, some incinerators in Europe are equipped with waste recovery, allowing the reuse of ferrous and non-ferrous materials found in landfills. A prominent example is the AEB Waste Fired Power Plant.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60">
       <a href="#cite_note-60">
        [60]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61">
       <a href="#cite_note-61">
        [61]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In Sweden, about 50% of the generated waste is burned in waste-to-energy facilities, producing electricity and supplying local cities' district heating systems.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62">
       <a href="#cite_note-62">
        [62]
       </a>
      </sup>
      The importance of waste in Sweden's electricity generation scheme is reflected on their 700.000 tons of waste imported per year to supply waste-to-energy facilities.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63">
       <a href="#cite_note-63">
        [63]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Incineration_in_the_United_Kingdom">
       Incineration in the United Kingdom
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Incineration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Incineration in the United Kingdom">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="hatnote" role="note">
      Main article:
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_incinerators_in_the_UK" title="List of incinerators in the UK">
       List of incinerators in the UK
      </a>
     </div>
     <p>
      The technology employed in the UK waste management industry has been greatly lagging behind that of Europe due to the wide availability of landfills. The
      <a href="/wiki/Landfill_Directive" title="Landfill Directive">
       Landfill Directive
      </a>
      set down by the
      <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">
       European Union
      </a>
      led to the Government of the United Kingdom imposing
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waste_legislation" title="Waste legislation">
       waste legislation
      </a>
      including the
      <a href="/wiki/Landfill_tax" title="Landfill tax">
       landfill tax
      </a>
      and
      <a href="/wiki/Landfill_Allowance_Trading_Scheme" title="Landfill Allowance Trading Scheme">
       Landfill Allowance Trading Scheme
      </a>
      . This legislation is designed to reduce the release of greenhouse gases produced by landfills through the use of alternative methods of waste treatment. It is the UK Government's position that incineration will play an increasingly large role in the treatment of municipal waste and supply of energy in the UK.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      In 2008, plans for potential incinerator locations exists for approximately 100 sites. These have been interactively mapped by UK NGO's.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64">
       <a href="#cite_note-64">
        [64]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65">
       <a href="#cite_note-65">
        [65]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66">
       <a href="#cite_note-66">
        [66]
       </a>
      </sup>
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67">
       <a href="#cite_note-67">
        [67]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Under a new plan in June 2012, a DEFRA-backed grant scheme (The Farming and Forestry Improvement Scheme) was set up to encourage the use of low-capacity incinerators on agricultural sites to improve their bio security.
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68">
       <a href="#cite_note-68">
        [68]
       </a>
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Incineration_Units_for_emergency_use">
       Incineration Units for emergency use
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Incineration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Incineration Units for emergency use">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tright">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Waste_Spectrum_HURIKAN_1000E_Mobile_Incineration_Unit.png">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1181" data-file-width="860" height="302" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Waste_Spectrum_HURIKAN_1000E_Mobile_Incineration_Unit.png/220px-Waste_Spectrum_HURIKAN_1000E_Mobile_Incineration_Unit.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Waste_Spectrum_HURIKAN_1000E_Mobile_Incineration_Unit.png/330px-Waste_Spectrum_HURIKAN_1000E_Mobile_Incineration_Unit.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Waste_Spectrum_HURIKAN_1000E_Mobile_Incineration_Unit.png/440px-Waste_Spectrum_HURIKAN_1000E_Mobile_Incineration_Unit.png 2x" width="220"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Waste_Spectrum_HURIKAN_1000E_Mobile_Incineration_Unit.png" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        Mobile incineration unit for emergency use
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Emergency incineration systems exist for the urgent and biosecure disposal of animals and their by-products following a mass mortality or disease outbreak. An increase in regulation and enforcement from governments and institutions worldwide has been forced through public pressure and significant economic exposure.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Contagious animal disease has cost governments and industry $200 billion over 20 years to 2012 and is responsible for over 65% of infectious disease outbreaks worldwide in the past sixty years. One-third of global meat exports (approx 6 million tonnes) is affected by trade restrictions at any time and as such the focus of Governments, public bodies and commercial operators is on cleaner, safer and more robust methods of animal carcass disposal to contain and control disease.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <p>
      Large-scale incineration systems are available from niche suppliers and are often bought by governments as a safety net in case of contagious outbreak. Many are mobile and can be quickly deployed to locations requiring biosecure disposal.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
     <h3>
      <span class="mw-headline" id="Small_incinerator_units">
       Small incinerator units
      </span>
      <span class="mw-editsection">
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        [
       </span>
       <a href="/w/index.php?title=Incineration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Small incinerator units">
        edit
       </a>
       <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
        ]
       </span>
      </span>
     </h3>
     <div class="thumb tleft">
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:277px;">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Incinerator_mobile.jpg">
        <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="225" data-file-width="300" height="206" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f4/Incinerator_mobile.jpg/275px-Incinerator_mobile.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/f/f4/Incinerator_mobile.jpg 1.5x" width="275"/>
       </a>
       <div class="thumbcaption">
        <div class="magnify">
         <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Incinerator_mobile.jpg" title="Enlarge">
         </a>
        </div>
        An example of a low capacity, mobile incinerator
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
     <p>
      Small-scale incinerators exist for special purposes. For example, the small-scale
      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69">
       <a href="#cite_note-69">
        [69]
       </a>
      </sup>
      incinerators are aimed for
      <a href="/wiki/Hygiene" title="Hygiene">
       hygienically
      </a>
      safe destruction of medical waste in
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developing_countries" title="Developing countries">
       developing countries
      </a>
      . Small incinerators can be quickly deployed to remote areas where an outbreak has occurred to dispose of infected animals quickly and without the risk of cross contamination.
      <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
       [
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
         <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2007)">
          citation needed
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       ]
      </sup>
     </p>
